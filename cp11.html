12  Argument, Justification, and Support

12.1 When Judgment Must Be Carried by Reasons

A judgment becomes stronger when it can be supported. The previous chapter studied how language moves from observation and evidence toward a grounded conclusion, showing that evidence does not automatically become judgment. This chapter continues that movement by asking how a judgment is supported once it has been formed. It studies argument, justification, and support: the language structures that explain why a conclusion should be accepted, trusted, acted upon, or revised.

A judgment without support may still be correct, but it remains fragile. It may be based on intuition, experience, or evidence not yet stated, but until the support is made visible, others cannot inspect it. Even the decision-maker may not fully understand why he believes what he believes. Deliberate language therefore asks: what is the judgment, why should it be accepted, what reasons support it, what evidence supports the reasons, what assumptions connect the evidence to the conclusion, what objections must be answered, and what limits does the justification have?

This chapter belongs to the middle of Part IV, where reasoning moves from evidence through argument toward justification. Argument gives judgment a backbone; justification makes judgment accountable. Together, they allow a conclusion to be examined before it is acted upon.

12.2 Argument as Structured Support

In ordinary speech, argument often means conflict. But in deliberate language, an argument is a structured set of reasons offered in support of a claim. It does not have to be aggressive, emotional, or involve two people fighting. A single person can make an argument privately when reasoning through a choice.

A basic argument contains three core elements: a claim, reasons, and support. The claim is the conclusion being asserted. The reasons are the statements offered in favor of the claim. The support is the evidence, criteria, assumptions, and reasoning that connect the reasons to the claim. For example: “I should choose Option B because it fits the long-term goal, produces stronger learning value, and keeps the downside manageable through staged commitment.” This is an argument containing a claim, multiple reasons, and an implicit support structure.

Reasons need relevance. A reason that is true but unrelated to the claim does not strengthen the argument. Showing that an option is cheaper is relevant only if cost is a valid criterion for the decision. Support needs strength. A reason supported by weak evidence is not much stronger than a reason without evidence. The quality of the argument depends on the quality of the support behind each reason.

A warrant is the logical connection that explains why a reason supports the claim. Sometimes the warrant is obvious; sometimes it must be made explicit. “The test scores improved, so the method works” assumes that improved test scores are a valid measure of learning. Making the warrant explicit allows it to be examined: is the test a fair measure, was the improvement significant, and could other factors explain the change?

12.3 Justification and Its Elements

Justification answers the question: why is this judgment acceptable? It goes beyond argument by considering the audience, the burden of proof, and the context in which the judgment must be held.

Justification differs from explanation. An explanation says why something happened. A justification says why a course of action is reasonable, acceptable, or preferable. “I chose Option B because I felt it was right” is an explanation of motivation but not a justification of reasoning. “I chose Option B because it better serves the long-term goal under the current constraints” is a justification that can be inspected.

The burden of justification falls on the person making the judgment. The more consequential the judgment, the heavier the burden. A judgment that affects other people, consumes significant resources, or is difficult to reverse requires stronger justification than a judgment with limited impact. The decision-maker must show not only that the conclusion is supported but also that the reasoning process was responsible.

Justification under uncertainty is especially demanding. When knowledge is incomplete, the justification must show that the decision is reasonable given what is known and what is not known. It must name the assumptions, acknowledge the limits of evidence, and explain why action is appropriate despite the gaps. A justification that hides uncertainty is not stronger; it is less trustworthy.

Objections must be answered in a complete justification. The strongest justifications anticipate objections and address them before they are raised. “One might argue that Option B is more expensive, but the additional cost is justified by the higher learning value and strategic fit.” This pattern makes the justification stronger by showing that the competing consideration has been recognized and weighed.

12.4 Argument and Justification Patterns

Good argument language names the claim, provides relevant reasons, supports those reasons with evidence, and makes the warrant explicit. It anticipates objections and acknowledges limits. It distinguishes between the strength of individual reasons and the strength of the overall case.

Bad argument language confuses assertion with argument: “This is the right choice” without reasons. It uses irrelevant reasons: true statements that do not support the claim. It relies on authority without supporting reasoning: “Because the expert said so.” It attacks the person instead of the argument. It presents many weak reasons as if quantity compensated for quality.

Good justification language acknowledges the burden, addresses the audience’s concerns, names assumptions and uncertainties, and shows why the conclusion is reasonable under the circumstances. Bad justification language hides assumptions, ignores uncertainty, or treats preference as justification.

A mature argument moves from evidence to conclusion through explicit reasoning steps. It shows the path, allows inspection, and remains open to counterargument. This does not make the argument weaker; it makes it accountable, which is the foundation of trust in reasoning.

12.5 Chapter Summary

This chapter has studied argument, justification, and support as the language structures that carry judgment from private conclusion to public accountability. Argument is structured support for a claim, containing a claim, reasons, and support with an explicit warrant connecting them. Justification answers why the judgment is acceptable, carrying a burden that increases with the consequence of the decision.

Argument and justification are not the same as explanation or assertion. Argument shows why a conclusion follows from its support; justification shows why the conclusion is reasonable for the audience and context. Good argument language makes reasoning visible; good justification language makes judgment accountable.

12.5.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied whenever you argue for or justify a judgment.

First, argument is not quarrel. An argument is a structured set of reasons in support of a claim. The strongest argument is not the loudest; it is the one whose reasoning is most visible and most testable. Second, every argument must contain three elements: a claim, reasons for the claim, and support that connects the reasons to evidence. A claim without reasons is an assertion; reasons without support are opinions. Third, make your warrant explicit. The warrant is the logical connection that explains why a reason supports the claim. When the warrant is hidden, the argument appears weaker because the reasoning step is invisible. Fourth, justification is not the same as explanation. An explanation says why something happened; a justification says why a choice is reasonable. When you are accountable for a decision, you owe others a justification, not merely an explanation of your motives. Fifth, the burden of justification increases with the consequence of the decision. A small personal choice may require little justification; a decision that affects others, consumes resources, or is difficult to reverse requires a proportionally stronger justification. Sixth, anticipate objections. The strongest justifications address counterarguments before they are raised, showing that competing considerations have been recognized and weighed. After argument and justification, Chapter 12 studies the third dimension of reasoning: how judgment is revised when conditions change or new evidence appears.

12.5.2 🎯 行动指南

今日练习：为你持有的一个立场写出完整论证

想一个你在最近讨论中坚持的立场（可以很小——比如”我们应该选方案A而不是方案B”）。写出来：

主张(Claim)：我主张_ 理由(Reasons)：因为_、____、____（列出2-3个理由） 支撑(Support)：每个理由背后的证据或逻辑是什么？____ 逻辑连接(Warrant)：为什么这个理由能支持这个主张？（比如：“因为成本是最重要的准则”或”因为过去的数据显示这个模式重复出现”）

然后，写下最强的反对意见： > 可能的反对：有人可能会说_ > 我的回应：_

日常行为调整： - 当你发现自己说”因为我觉得”时，追问自己：这是解释动机还是正当化选择？ - 下次讨论中，如果有人只说断言不给理由，温和地问：“为什么你这么认为？” - 在重要沟通中（写邮件、提建议、做汇报），先列理由再说主张——不要先说结论再补充理由。 - 做一个”异议前置”练习：在提出一个建议之前，先说出它的最大弱点。这会让你看起来更可信，而不是更弱。
