6  Refusing, Promising, and Committing

6.1 Language and Responsibility

Some speech acts do more than exchange information.

They create responsibility.

When a person says:

“No.”

they draw a boundary.

When a person says:

“I promise.”

they create an expectation.

When a person says:

“I will take care of it.”

they accept an obligation.

These utterances are not merely descriptions of inner states. They change the social situation.

Before the sentence is spoken, responsibility may be undefined.
After the sentence is spoken, responsibility may become assigned, limited, rejected, or accepted.

This chapter examines three closely related speech actions:

Refusing
Promising
Committing

They matter because human cooperation depends on the management of responsibility.

People must know:

  • what they can ask from others

  • what others can expect from them

  • what has been accepted

  • what has been declined

  • what remains open

  • what is now binding

Without clear refusal, boundaries collapse.
Without reliable promises, trust collapses.
Without explicit commitment, coordination collapses.

Language is the mechanism through which these social structures are created.

6.2 From Action to Obligation

Chapter 4 examined informing, asking, and directing.

Those speech acts move knowledge and action.

This chapter moves deeper into responsibility.

A direction says:

“Do this.”

A promise says:

“I will do this.”

A refusal says:

“I will not do this.”

A commitment says:

“You can rely on me for this.”

These are not minor differences.

They define who carries the burden of future action.

For example:

“Can you send the report tonight?”

This is a request.

The listener may answer:

“Yes, I will send it by 8 p.m.”

Now the speech situation has changed.

The listener has created an expectation.
The speaker can now plan around that expectation.
If the report is not sent, trust may be damaged.

A commitment is therefore not only a statement about future behavior.

It is a social bridge between present speech and future action.

6.3 Refusing

Refusing is the speech act of rejecting a request, offer, expectation, role, obligation, or proposed action.

Its basic structure is:

Speaker → Boundary → Listener

A refusal says:

This will not be accepted.
This will not be done.
This will not continue.
This responsibility will not be taken.
This expectation will not be granted.

Examples:

“No.”
“I cannot do that.”
“I am not available tomorrow.”
“I cannot agree to these terms.”
“I will not take responsibility for that decision.”
“This is outside the scope of my role.”
“I appreciate the offer, but I have to decline.”

Refusal is one of the most important forms of pragmatic language because it protects boundaries.

A person who cannot refuse clearly will often accept responsibilities they cannot carry.

A team that cannot refuse scope will lose control of its work.

An organization that cannot refuse bad opportunities will lose strategic direction.

Refusal is not merely negative.

It is a structural act.

It preserves capacity, priority, integrity, and responsibility.

6.4 What Refusal Does

A refusal performs several operations at once.

It rejects an expectation.
It defines a boundary.
It prevents false agreement.
It protects time, energy, money, attention, or values.
It may preserve trust by preventing overcommitment.
It may create disappointment, conflict, or negotiation.

For example:

“I cannot finish this by Friday.”

This refusal may disappoint the requester.

But if it is true, saying it clearly is more responsible than saying:

“I will try.”

when the speaker already knows the deadline is unrealistic.

A clear refusal may cause short-term discomfort.

An unclear non-refusal may cause long-term failure.

Refusal is therefore not the opposite of cooperation.

Often, refusal is what makes honest cooperation possible.

6.5 Refusal and Boundary

Every refusal contains a boundary.

The boundary may concern:

  • time

  • role

  • capacity

  • authority

  • values

  • responsibility

  • scope

  • risk

  • consent

  • priority

Examples:

“I do not have time this week.”
“This is not my area of responsibility.”
“I cannot approve this without evidence.”
“I am not comfortable with that.”
“We cannot expand the scope without changing the deadline.”
“I cannot promise a result I do not control.”

A strong refusal makes the boundary visible.

A weak refusal leaves the boundary ambiguous.

For example:

“Maybe later.”

This may be polite, but it can create false expectation.

If the real meaning is no, a clearer version is better:

“I cannot take this on this month.”

or:

“I do not think I am the right person for this.”

The goal is not to be harsh.

The goal is to prevent the listener from building plans around a false possibility.

6.6 Types of Refusal

Refusal can take different forms depending on the situation.

Type Function Example
Direct Refusal Clearly rejects “No, I cannot do that.”
Capacity Refusal Refuses due to limited resources “I do not have time this week.”
Scope Refusal Refuses because request exceeds agreement “That is outside the project scope.”
Authority Refusal Refuses because speaker lacks power “I cannot approve that decision.”
Value Refusal Refuses because of principle “I am not willing to do that.”
Risk Refusal Refuses because consequence is too high “We should not launch while this bug remains unresolved.”
Conditional Refusal Refuses under current conditions “Not unless the timeline changes.”
Redirecting Refusal Refuses while offering alternative path “I cannot review the whole draft, but I can review the introduction.”

These forms are not identical.

A direct refusal closes the path.

A conditional refusal leaves a possible path open.

A redirecting refusal preserves cooperation while limiting responsibility.

6.7 Refusal Is Not Always Rejection of the Person

One major difficulty in refusal is relational interpretation.

When someone hears:

“No.”

they may feel:

“You rejected me.”

But a refusal may reject only the request, not the person.

This distinction often needs to be made explicit.

For example:

“I value the project, but I cannot take on this extra section without delaying my current work.”

This separates:

  • respect for the project

  • refusal of the additional task

  • reason for refusal

  • consequence of accepting

Another example:

“I appreciate the invitation, but I cannot attend this time.”

This refuses the event, not the relationship.

In fragile relationships, refusal should often include relational care.

But relational care should not erase the boundary.

A refusal that sounds kind but remains unclear may fail as a refusal.

A good refusal can be both respectful and firm.

6.8 The Structure of a Responsible Refusal

A responsible refusal often contains five parts:

Recognition
Boundary
Reason
Alternative, if available
Closure

6.8.1 Recognition

Acknowledge the request or situation.

“I understand why this is important.”

6.8.2 Boundary

State the refusal clearly.

“I cannot take this on.”

6.8.3 Reason

Give enough explanation to make the refusal intelligible.

“My current deadline already requires full attention this week.”

6.8.4 Alternative

Offer another path only if it is real.

“I can review one section on Thursday, or we can ask someone else to take the full review.”

6.8.5 Closure

Prevent false ambiguity.

“But I cannot own the full task.”

A full example:

“I understand this review is urgent. I cannot take on the full document this week because my current delivery is already at capacity. I can review the introduction on Thursday, or we can find another reviewer for the full draft. But I cannot be responsible for the complete review.”

This refusal is clear, bounded, and cooperative.

6.9 Bad Refusals

Refusal can fail in several ways.

6.9.1 The Hidden Refusal

The speaker intends to refuse but does not say so clearly.

“I will see.”
“Maybe.”
“Let me think about it.”
“I might be able to.”

These may be honest if uncertainty is real.

But if the speaker already knows the answer is no, these phrases create false expectation.

6.9.2 The Over-Explained Refusal

The speaker gives too much justification and weakens the boundary.

“I am so sorry, I really wanted to help, but there are many reasons, and I feel bad…”

Excess explanation may invite negotiation when the boundary is already fixed.

6.9.3 The Aggressive Refusal

The speaker protects the boundary but damages the relationship unnecessarily.

“That is not my problem.”

Sometimes this may be true, but it often creates avoidable conflict.

6.9.4 The Ambiguous Conditional Refusal

The speaker refuses under certain conditions but fails to define them.

“Not now.”

Does that mean later today, next week, next month, or never?

A clearer version:

“I cannot do this before Friday. If it can wait until next week, I can reconsider.”

6.9.5 The False Alternative

The speaker offers an alternative that is not real.

“Maybe someone else can help.”

This may sound cooperative but gives no actual path.

Better:

“I cannot help, and I do not know who is available.”

Honest limitation is better than decorative helpfulness.

6.10 Promising

Promising is the speech act of creating expectation about future action.

Its basic structure is:

Speaker → Future Action → Listener

A promise says:

You may expect this from me.

Examples:

“I will call you tomorrow.”
“I promise I will be there.”
“We will deliver the report by Friday.”
“I will not share this information.”
“I will take responsibility for the next version.”

A promise binds speech to future behavior.

It changes what the listener may reasonably expect.

This is why promises are powerful.

They allow people to coordinate across time.

Without promises, cooperation would be fragile.

People could only respond to what is happening now. They could not reliably build plans around future action.

A promise creates continuity between present trust and future conduct.

6.11 The Structure of a Promise

A strong promise usually includes:

Action
Owner
Time
Condition
Standard
Consequence

For example:

“I will send you the revised draft by 6 p.m. tomorrow, with the introduction rewritten and the evidence table updated.”

This promise defines:

  • owner: I

  • action: send the revised draft

  • time: by 6 p.m. tomorrow

  • standard: introduction rewritten, evidence table updated

A weak promise:

“I will handle it.”

This may be acceptable if the context is already shared.

But if context is unclear, it leaves too much undefined.

What does “handle” mean?
By when?
To what standard?
With what result?
Who will be informed?

A promise becomes stronger when the expected future is concrete enough to be checked.

6.12 Promise and Trust

Promises create trust when they are fulfilled.

They damage trust when they are broken.

But the damage is not only caused by the missing action.

It is caused by the collapse of expectation.

If someone says:

“I will send it tonight.”

the listener may plan around that promise.

They may delay their own work.
They may tell others the task is covered.
They may stop searching for alternatives.
They may rely on the speaker.

If the promise fails, the harm includes all downstream dependencies.

This is why careless promising is dangerous.

A person may promise casually because the sentence feels small.

But the expectation created by the promise may become large.

Responsible promising requires awareness of dependency.

Before promising, ask:

  • Do I control the action?

  • Do I control the timing?

  • Do I understand the standard?

  • Do I have the capacity?

  • What will others rely on?

  • What happens if I fail?

  • Should this be a promise, an intention, or a possibility?

Not all future-oriented speech should become a promise.

6.13 Promise, Intention, and Hope

A major pragmatic error is confusing promise with intention or hope.

These are different.

Form Meaning Example
Hope Desired future, low control “I hope to finish it soon.”
Intention Planned future, some control “I intend to finish it this week.”
Commitment Accepted responsibility “I will finish it by Friday.”
Promise Strong interpersonal commitment “I promise I will finish it by Friday.”

The difference matters.

If a speaker says:

“I hope I can help.”

the listener should not treat this as a promise.

If a speaker says:

“I will help.”

the listener may reasonably expect action.

If a speaker says:

“I promise I will help.”

the expectation becomes stronger, and failure becomes more serious.

A responsible speaker should choose the correct level of commitment.

Do not use promise language for uncertain possibilities.

Do not use vague intention when others need commitment.

6.14 Commitment

Commitment is broader than promise.

A promise is usually made to someone.

A commitment may be made to a person, group, role, value, plan, goal, standard, or future course of action.

Examples:

“I will lead this workstream.”
“We are committed to finishing this project.”
“I take responsibility for the final review.”
“From now on, all releases must pass this checklist.”
“We will prioritize reliability over speed in this phase.”

Commitment organizes future behavior.

It tells others:

This is the direction we will hold.
This is the responsibility we accept.
This is the standard we will follow.
This is the role we will occupy.

Commitment is essential for coordination because it stabilizes expectation.

Without commitment, people may talk endlessly but never bind themselves to action.

6.15 Commitment as Role Acceptance

Many commitments are role commitments.

To accept a role is to accept a bundle of expectations.

For example:

“I will be the project owner.”

This means more than:

“I will do some tasks.”

It may imply responsibility for:

  • progress

  • coordination

  • communication

  • risk tracking

  • decision escalation

  • delivery

  • accountability

If the role is not defined, the commitment is unstable.

A person may say:

“I will own this.”

But “own” may mean different things:

  • I will execute the task.

  • I will coordinate others.

  • I will make decisions.

  • I will report progress.

  • I will be accountable for the result.

  • I will make sure someone handles it.

A responsible commitment defines the role.

Better:

“I will own the final review. That means I will collect comments, resolve conflicts, check the final version, and send the approval note by Friday.”

This turns role language into operational commitment.

6.16 Commitment as Boundary

Commitment does not only say yes.

It also says no to competing possibilities.

To commit to one path is to refuse other paths.

For example:

“We will prioritize quality over speed.”

This commitment means:

We will not sacrifice quality merely to move faster.

Another example:

“I will focus on IELTS preparation for the next three months.”

This commitment means:

I will reduce or reject activities that compete with that priority.

A commitment is meaningful only if it constrains future action.

If a commitment does not change what one will refuse, it is weak.

This is why commitment and refusal are structurally linked.

To commit is to accept one responsibility and reject others.

6.17 Explicit and Implicit Commitment

Commitments can be explicit or implicit.

Explicit commitment:

“I will send the proposal by Friday.”

Implicit commitment:

The speaker repeatedly behaves as if they are responsible, and others begin to rely on them.

Implicit commitments are common in families, teams, friendships, and organizations.

For example, if one person always organizes the meeting notes, others may begin to assume:

“This person owns the notes.”

Even if no one said it directly.

Implicit commitment can support smooth coordination, but it can also create hidden burden.

A person may become responsible for something without ever agreeing to it.

This often causes resentment.

A useful pragmatic practice is to make implicit commitments explicit.

For example:

“It seems I have been handling the notes by default. Should I officially own this, or should we rotate the responsibility?”

This sentence exposes hidden responsibility.

It allows renegotiation.

6.18 Conditional Commitment

Not all commitments are absolute.

Many responsible commitments are conditional.

Examples:

“I can finish this by Friday if I receive the data by Wednesday.”
“We can launch next week if testing passes.”
“I will join the project if my role is clearly defined.”
“I can support the plan if the budget risk is addressed.”

Conditional commitment is often stronger than vague agreement.

It clarifies what must be true for the commitment to hold.

A weak version:

“Yes, we can probably do it.”

A stronger version:

“We can do it by Friday if scope remains unchanged and design approval comes by Tuesday.”

This protects coordination.

It prevents people from treating a conditional possibility as an unconditional promise.

6.19 Commitment and Capacity

A commitment is only responsible if it respects capacity.

Capacity includes:

  • time

  • attention

  • skill

  • authority

  • resources

  • emotional energy

  • decision power

  • control over dependencies

A person may sincerely want to commit but lack capacity.

For example:

“I will finish the whole project this week.”

This may be emotionally sincere but operationally false.

Good commitment requires capacity checking.

Before committing, ask:

What exactly am I accepting?
Do I control the required actions?
Do I have the time?
Do I have the skill?
Do I have the authority?
What dependencies could block me?
What standard must be met?
What happens if I cannot deliver?

If capacity is uncertain, use calibrated language:

“I can commit to completing the first draft by Friday. I cannot yet commit to the final version until I know how much revision will be required.”

This is more responsible than broad enthusiasm.

6.20 Overcommitment

Overcommitment occurs when accepted responsibility exceeds real capacity.

It often comes from:

  • desire to please

  • fear of refusal

  • unclear scope

  • underestimating difficulty

  • pressure from authority

  • optimism bias

  • lack of boundary language

  • confusion between intention and commitment

Overcommitment damages trust because it creates expectations that cannot be fulfilled.

A person may believe they are being generous by saying yes.

But if the yes is unrealistic, it becomes a future failure.

A responsible speaker must sometimes refuse in order to protect real commitment.

For example:

“I cannot take on the additional chapter this week. If I accept it, I will risk delaying the current chapter. I can either finish the current chapter well, or switch priorities, but I cannot responsibly do both.”

This is not weakness.

It is accurate commitment management.

6.21 Undercommitment

Undercommitment is the opposite failure.

It occurs when a person avoids responsibility even when commitment is needed.

Forms of undercommitment include:

“I will try.”
“Maybe.”
“Let us see.”
“I might be able to.”
“It should be fine.”
“Someone will handle it.”

Sometimes these phrases are appropriate because reality is uncertain.

But when the situation requires ownership, they are too weak.

For example, in a team setting:

“Someone should check the final numbers.”

This does not assign responsibility.

Better:

“I will check the final numbers by 4 p.m.”

or:

“Li will check the final numbers, and I will review them before sending.”

Undercommitment creates coordination gaps.

Everyone may assume someone else is responsible.

Clear commitment closes the gap.

6.22 Broken Promises and Repair

Promises may fail.

Commitments may become impossible.

The question is not only whether failure happens, but how the speaker handles it.

A broken promise without repair damages trust deeply.

A responsible repair should include:

Acknowledgment
Responsibility
Explanation
Impact
Correction
Future prevention

Example:

“I said I would send the report by 6 p.m., and I did not. That created a problem for your review schedule. The delay happened because I underestimated the data cleanup, but that was my responsibility to communicate earlier. I can send the completed version by 10 a.m. tomorrow, and next time I will flag this kind of delay as soon as I see it.”

This repair does not hide behind excuses.

It names:

  • the original commitment

  • the failure

  • the impact

  • the cause

  • the responsibility

  • the correction

  • the prevention

A weak repair:

“Sorry, things got busy.”

This may be emotionally polite, but structurally insufficient.

When trust is damaged, repair must address the expectation that was broken.

6.23 Refusal, Promise, and Commitment Compared

These three speech acts can be compared by how they manage responsibility.

Speech Act Basic Function Responsibility Effect Example
Refusing Rejects expectation or obligation Limits responsibility “I cannot do that.”
Promising Creates expectation of future action Accepts responsibility “I will do that by Friday.”
Committing Binds speaker to role, plan, value, or action Stabilizes responsibility over time “I will own this workstream.”

Refusal protects the boundary of responsibility.

Promise creates a specific expectation.

Commitment stabilizes a future pattern of responsibility.

All three are necessary.

Without refusal, responsibility expands without limit.
Without promise, future action cannot be trusted.
Without commitment, plans lack ownership.

6.24 The Social Weight of Speech

Refusing, promising, and committing have social weight because they alter expectation.

After refusal, the listener must seek another path.

After promise, the listener may rely on the speaker.

After commitment, others may organize around the speaker’s role.

This is why these speech acts require care.

They affect:

  • trust

  • planning

  • coordination

  • emotional safety

  • role clarity

  • future accountability

A person who refuses carelessly may appear unreliable or cold.

A person who promises carelessly may appear trustworthy in the moment but unreliable over time.

A person who commits vaguely may create confusion while appearing cooperative.

Pragmatic maturity requires calibration.

Say no when the answer is no.
Say yes only when yes can be carried.
Define commitment when others must rely on it.

6.25 The Grammar of Refusal

A practical refusal can follow this structure:

I understand [request / need / situation].
I cannot [accept / do / agree to X].
The reason is [capacity / role / value / constraint].
What I can do is [alternative], if available.
So the boundary is [clear limit].

Example:

“I understand that the deadline is tight. I cannot take on the full revision tonight because I already have another delivery due tomorrow. What I can do is review the problem statement and leave comments by 9 p.m. But I cannot own the full rewrite.”

This refusal is clear and cooperative.

It does not hide the no.

It also does not turn the no into unnecessary hostility.

6.26 The Grammar of Promise

A practical promise can follow this structure:

I will [action]
by [time]
to [standard / result]
under [conditions, if any].
If that changes, I will [communication / repair action].

Example:

“I will send the revised chapter by Friday evening, with the introduction rewritten and the examples cleaned up. If I see by Thursday that the timeline is at risk, I will tell you immediately rather than waiting until the deadline.”

This promise includes action, time, standard, and communication responsibility.

It creates a reliable expectation.

6.27 The Grammar of Commitment

A practical commitment can follow this structure:

I accept responsibility for [role / task / outcome].
This includes [specific responsibilities].
This does not include [boundaries].
The success standard is [criteria].
The next checkpoint is [time / review point].

Example:

“I accept responsibility for the final review of this document. This includes collecting comments, resolving conflicts, checking consistency, and sending the approval version. It does not include rewriting every chapter from scratch. The success standard is that the document is coherent, complete, and ready for publication review. The next checkpoint is Thursday afternoon.”

This makes the commitment operational.

It prevents role language from becoming vague burden.

6.28 Failure Modes

6.28.1 Refusing Too Late

The speaker delays refusal until others have already relied on them.

“Actually, I cannot do it.”

This causes more damage than an early refusal.

Early refusal allows replanning.

Late refusal creates crisis.

6.28.2 Promising Too Early

The speaker promises before understanding scope.

“Yes, I can handle it.”

Later, the task turns out to be larger than expected.

A better response:

“I need to understand the scope before I commit.”

6.28.3 Committing Without Boundaries

The speaker accepts responsibility without defining limits.

“I will own everything.”

This may sound strong, but it is structurally dangerous.

Ownership must have scope.

6.28.4 Refusing Without Care

The speaker protects the boundary but ignores relationship.

“No. Not my problem.”

This may be efficient, but it may damage trust unnecessarily.

6.28.5 Promising Without Capacity

The speaker creates expectation without resources.

“I will finish all of it tonight.”

Sincerity does not make the promise responsible.

6.28.6 Commitment Without Checkpoint

The speaker accepts responsibility but creates no review point.

“I will take care of it.”

No one knows when progress will be checked.

Coordination becomes invisible.

6.28.7 Ambiguous Yes

The speaker says yes, but means only partial agreement.

“Sure.”

Does this mean agreement, willingness, full commitment, or polite acknowledgment?

A clearer version:

“Yes, I can help with the outline, but I cannot write the full draft.”

6.29 Practical Diagnostic Questions

Before refusing, ask:

What exactly am I refusing?
Am I refusing the person, the request, the timing, the scope, or the condition?
Does the listener need an alternative?
Is my boundary clear?
Am I leaving false hope?

Before promising, ask:

What exactly am I promising?
Can I control the result?
Can I meet the time and standard?
Who will rely on this promise?
What should I say if conditions change?

Before committing, ask:

What responsibility am I accepting?
What is included?
What is excluded?
What standard defines success?
What dependencies affect delivery?
When will this be checked?

These questions turn speech from impulse into responsibility design.

6.30 Minimal Operational Distinction

A simple distinction can guide real communication:

Refusing: I will not take this responsibility.
Promising: You can expect this future action from me.
Committing: I accept this responsibility over time.

This distinction helps avoid vague speech.

If the answer is no, refuse clearly.

If the answer is yes, define the promise.

If the responsibility continues over time, define the commitment.

6.31 Chapter Summary

Refusing, promising, and committing are speech acts that manage responsibility.

Refusal rejects or limits responsibility.
Promise creates expectation about future action.
Commitment stabilizes responsibility across time, role, plan, or value.

These speech acts are central to pragmatic language because they shape trust, coordination, obligation, and social reality.

A refusal protects boundaries.
A promise creates reliance.
A commitment creates ownership.

Each must be designed carefully.

Bad refusal creates confusion or unnecessary damage.
Bad promise creates false expectation.
Bad commitment creates hidden burden or unclear accountability.

The central discipline is this:

Do not let responsibility remain vague.

When language accepts responsibility, define it.
When language rejects responsibility, clarify the boundary.
When language creates expectation, make it reliable.

In this way, speech becomes not merely expression, but a structure of trust and action.
