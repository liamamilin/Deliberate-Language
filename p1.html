Part I — Problems, Goals, and Decision Space

Orientation

Deliberate thinking does not begin with comparison or judgment.

It begins earlier.

Before any evaluation can take place, three conditions must be established:

  • the problem must be defined

  • the goal must be clarified

  • the space of possible options must be constructed

Without these, judgment has no stable object,
and strategy has no direction.

The Entry Point of Deliberation

Every decision operates within a structured space.

This space is not given by reality itself.
It is constructed through language.

Different formulations of:

  • what the problem is

  • what counts as success

  • what alternatives exist

will produce entirely different decision paths.

A change in language at this stage is not cosmetic.
It is structural.

Three Foundational Constructions

This part focuses on three foundational constructions:

Problem

The problem defines:

  • what is being attended to

  • what is considered relevant

  • what is excluded

A poorly defined problem makes correct judgment impossible,
regardless of reasoning quality.

Goal

The goal defines:

  • what counts as success

  • what criteria will be used

  • what trade-offs are acceptable

Without explicit goals, comparison collapses into preference.

Decision Space

The decision space defines:

  • what options are visible

  • what alternatives are imaginable

  • what possibilities are ignored

A narrow decision space leads to false necessity.
An unstructured one leads to confusion.

Structural Dependency

These three elements are not independent.

They form a dependency structure:

Problem → Goal → Decision Space

  • how the problem is framed constrains the goals

  • how the goals are defined shapes the space of options

  • how options are constructed feeds back into problem understanding

Deliberation is therefore not linear.

It is an iterative refinement of these three structures.

Failure Modes at the Entry Point

Most decision failures originate here.

Common failure modes include:

  • treating symptoms as problems

  • defining goals without criteria

  • collapsing multiple goals into one

  • assuming a fixed set of options

  • ignoring alternative framings

  • failing to expand the decision space

These are not errors of logic.

They are errors of construction.

Function of This Part

This part establishes the foundation for all subsequent reasoning.

It provides the tools to:

  • define problems with precision

  • construct goals with explicit criteria

  • generate and structure decision spaces

Only after these are in place can comparison, trade-off, and judgment become meaningful.

Transition

Once multiple options exist within a defined decision space,
deliberation enters its next phase:

comparison, trade-off, and evaluation.
