8  Uncertainty and Partial Knowledge

8.1 The Space Between Knowing and Not Knowing

Deliberate judgment often begins in an uncomfortable condition: we know something, but not enough.

We may know the problem, but not the full cause.
We may know the goal, but not the path.
We may know the options, but not their consequences.
We may know the evidence, but not how stable it is.
We may know what happened, but not what it means.

This is the condition of partial knowledge.

The previous part studied comparison, trade-off, and evaluation. It showed how language helps alternatives become comparable, how competing values become visible, and how evaluation becomes possible under explicit criteria. This chapter begins Part III by moving into a harder condition: evaluation when knowledge is incomplete. Here, language must represent what is known, what is unclear, what is assumed, and what remains unknown.

Uncertainty is not the absence of thought. It is one of the main conditions under which thought must operate.

A person who cannot speak about uncertainty clearly will usually fall into one of two errors.

The first error is false certainty:

I know this will work.
This is definitely the right path.
There is no problem here.
The outcome is obvious.

The second error is vague doubt:

I do not know.
Everything is unclear.
This is too uncertain.
We cannot decide.

Both are weak forms of language.

False certainty hides what is not known.
Vague doubt hides what is already known.

Deliberate language must do something more precise. It must divide the field of knowledge.

It must be able to say:

This part is known.
This part is unclear.
This part is assumed.
This part is unknown.
This part can be tested.
This part cannot be known yet.
This part is uncertain but not decisive.
This part is uncertain and central to the decision.

This chapter studies that language.

8.2 Partial Knowledge Is Structured Knowledge

Partial knowledge does not mean chaos.

It means that knowledge is unevenly distributed across a situation. Some parts are clear. Some parts are unclear. Some parts are supported by evidence. Some parts are guesses. Some parts are missing entirely.

A mature thinker does not simply say:

I do not know enough.

He asks:

Which part do I know?
Which part do I not know?
Which part matters most?
Which unknown blocks the decision?
Which unknown can be tolerated?
Which unknown can be reduced through action?

This is the first function of uncertainty language: to structure incomplete knowledge.

For example, consider this weak statement:

I do not know whether this plan will work.

This statement expresses uncertainty, but it does not organize it.

A stronger version says:

We know the team has the technical ability. We do not know whether users actually need this feature. We also do not know whether the current timeline is realistic. The most important unknown is user demand.

Now the uncertainty has structure.

The speaker has separated:

  • known capability

  • unknown demand

  • unknown timeline

  • most important unknown

This makes the situation discussable. It also shows where investigation should begin.

Partial knowledge becomes useful when language gives it shape.

8.3 Known, Unknown, Assumed, and Unclear

Deliberate language must distinguish at least four categories:

Known
Unknown
Assumed
Unclear

These words may appear simple, but they perform different cognitive functions.

8.4 The Known

The known refers to information that is supported by evidence, direct observation, reliable records, repeated experience, or strong verification.

Examples:

We know the deadline is June 30.
We know the current budget is limited.
We know this method worked in the previous project.
We know the user complained about loading speed.
We know the team has two available engineers.

Known information provides the ground of judgment.

But even known information has scope. A fact may be true within one context and not automatically transferable to another.

For example:

We know this worked last year.

This does not mean:

We know this will work again.

The first sentence is evidence.
The second sentence is a forecast.

Deliberate language prevents evidence from being overstretched.

A stronger formulation says:

We know this worked last year under similar conditions, but we do not yet know whether the current conditions are similar enough.

This sentence protects the boundary of knowledge.

8.5 The Unknown

The unknown refers to information not currently available.

Examples:

We do not know how users will respond.
We do not know whether the cost will increase.
We do not know whether the partner will cooperate.
We do not know how long the transition will take.
We do not know whether the opportunity will still exist in six months.

Unknowns matter because they indicate gaps in the decision structure.

But not all unknowns are equally important.

Some unknowns are irrelevant.
Some are interesting but not decisive.
Some are central.
Some are dangerous.
Some are impossible to know in advance.
Some can be reduced through research, testing, or staged action.

A weak statement says:

There are too many unknowns.

A stronger statement says:

There are three unknowns. Two are manageable. One is central: whether we can sustain the cost for twelve months.

This is a major improvement. It turns vague anxiety into a structured problem.

8.6 The Assumed

The assumed refers to what is being treated as true for the purpose of reasoning, even though it is not fully proven.

Examples:

We assume demand will continue.
We assume the team can learn the tool quickly.
We assume the market will not change dramatically.
We assume the current motivation can be sustained.
We assume this person’s advice is reliable.

Assumptions are not necessarily bad. No decision can operate without them. The problem is not assumption itself. The problem is hidden assumption.

An explicit assumption can be examined.

A hidden assumption quietly controls the judgment.

For example:

This plan will probably work.

Behind this sentence may be several assumptions:

  • the user need is real

  • the team can execute

  • the cost is manageable

  • the timeline is realistic

  • no major dependency will fail

  • the environment will remain stable

Deliberate language brings these assumptions into view:

This plan depends on the assumption that user demand is strong enough and that the team can deliver within the current timeline.

Now the reasoning has a visible foundation.

8.7 The Unclear

The unclear refers to information that exists in some form but is not yet understood, interpreted, or defined clearly.

Examples:

The feedback is unclear.
The goal is unclear.
The cause of failure is unclear.
The stakeholder’s real concern is unclear.
The meaning of “success” is unclear.
The relation between cost and benefit is unclear.

Unclear is different from unknown.

Unknown means the information is missing.
Unclear means the information is present but not yet intelligible.

For example:

We do not know what users think.

This is an unknown.

But:

Users gave feedback, but the feedback is inconsistent and hard to interpret.

This is unclear.

The response should be different. Unknown information may require collection. Unclear information may require interpretation, classification, or clarification.

Deliberate language asks:

Is this missing information, or unclear information?
Do we need more data, or better interpretation?
Do we need investigation, or definition?
Do we lack facts, or do we lack structure?

This distinction prevents wasted effort. Sometimes people collect more information when what they actually need is clearer categories. Sometimes they debate interpretation when what they actually need is missing evidence.

8.8 Degrees of Confidence

Knowledge is not always binary.

Between certainty and ignorance, there are degrees of confidence.

A deliberate language system needs words and structures for these degrees:

  • certain

  • highly likely

  • likely

  • plausible

  • possible

  • uncertain

  • doubtful

  • unlikely

  • unknown

These are not decorative words. They help prevent overstatement.

Compare these sentences:

This will work.

This is likely to work if the current assumptions hold.

The second sentence is less forceful, but more responsible. It marks both confidence and dependency.

Confidence language should also be connected to evidence:

We are highly confident because this pattern has repeated across several cases.
We are moderately confident because the evidence is suggestive but incomplete.
We have low confidence because the claim depends mostly on one untested assumption.

This kind of language allows judgment without pretending to certainty.

In many serious situations, the goal is not to become perfectly certain. The goal is to know whether confidence is high enough for the type of action being considered.

A reversible experiment may require only moderate confidence.
A major irreversible commitment may require high confidence.
A safety-critical decision may require extremely strong evidence.
A low-cost exploration may be acceptable under uncertainty.

Therefore, confidence language must connect to commitment level.

We do not need full certainty to test this.
We need stronger evidence before committing fully.
Current confidence is enough for exploration, but not enough for irreversible action.

This is mature uncertainty language.

8.9 The Boundary of Knowledge

One of the most important functions of deliberate language is to mark the boundary of knowledge.

A knowledge boundary tells us where evidence ends and inference begins.

For example:

We know the user stopped using the product after three days.

That is an observation.

The user stopped because the product was confusing.

That is an interpretation.

The interpretation may be correct, but it is not the same as the observation.

Deliberate language separates them:

We know the user stopped after three days. We infer that confusion may be one cause, but we do not yet have direct evidence.

This sentence is stronger because it protects the boundary between fact and explanation.

A similar distinction appears between:

  • observation

  • interpretation

  • assumption

  • prediction

  • judgment

For example:

Observation: Sales declined for three months.
Interpretation: Customers may be losing interest.
Assumption: The decline reflects demand, not distribution problems.
Prediction: Sales may continue declining.
Judgment: We should investigate customer retention before expanding marketing spend.

Each layer has a different epistemic status. If language collapses them, the decision becomes fragile.

Weak language says:

Customers do not want this anymore.

Stronger language says:

Sales declined for three months. One possible interpretation is weakening demand, but we need to check whether distribution, pricing, or product issues explain the decline.

The second version keeps thought open without losing direction.

8.10 What Remains Unknown

A mature decision does not merely state what is known. It also states what remains unknown.

This is important because unspoken unknowns often become future shocks.

Useful language patterns include:

What we still do not know is…
The main unknown is…
The unknown that matters most is…
This decision remains exposed to…
The unresolved question is…
We cannot yet determine whether…
This conclusion may change if…

These patterns create space for revision.

For example:

We can proceed with the prototype, but we still do not know whether users will pay for the full product.

This sentence supports action while preserving awareness.

It does not say:

Everything is clear.

It also does not say:

We cannot act.

It says:

We can act within the boundary of what we know.

This is the basic posture of deliberate action under partial knowledge.

8.11 Unknown Does Not Always Mean Stop

One of the most common mistakes is treating every unknown as a stop sign.

But unknowns have different decision implications.

Some unknowns require stopping:

We do not know whether this action is legal.

Some unknowns require research:

We do not know the actual cost.

Some unknowns require testing:

We do not know whether users want this feature.

Some unknowns require monitoring:

We do not know whether the external environment will change.

Some unknowns require fallback:

We do not know whether the primary partner will deliver on time.

Some unknowns can be accepted:

We do not know the exact outcome, but the downside is limited and reversible.

Deliberate language must therefore classify unknowns by response.

A weak sentence says:

There is uncertainty, so we should not proceed.

A stronger sentence says:

The uncertainty is real, but it is testable and the downside is limited, so a small experiment is justified.

Or:

The uncertainty concerns a non-reversible legal exposure, so we should not proceed until it is clarified.

The issue is not whether uncertainty exists. The issue is what kind of uncertainty it is and what kind of action it permits.

8.12 Testable and Untestable Unknowns

Some unknowns can be reduced before the decision. Others can only be resolved through action.

A testable unknown can be investigated, measured, simulated, prototyped, or clarified.

Examples:

We can test user interest with a landing page.
We can estimate cost through supplier quotes.
We can clarify requirements through stakeholder interviews.
We can run a small pilot before full rollout.
We can compare historical cases.

An untestable or not-yet-testable unknown cannot be fully resolved in advance.

Examples:

We cannot know exactly how the market will look in three years.
We cannot know whether motivation will remain constant for a long period.
We cannot know all future consequences of this career move.
We cannot know how every stakeholder will respond.

When an unknown is testable, the proper response is often learning before commitment.

When an unknown is not fully testable, the proper response is often robustness, reversibility, and adaptation.

Deliberate language asks:

Can this unknown be tested before commitment?
If not, can the decision be made reversible?
Can we reduce the downside?
Can we create checkpoints?
Can we monitor early signals?
Can we preserve a fallback path?

This kind of language does not demand impossible certainty. It designs action around the limits of knowledge.

8.13 The Difference Between Uncertainty and Ambiguity

Uncertainty means that we do not know which outcome will occur.

Ambiguity means that the meaning of the situation itself is unclear.

For example:

We do not know whether the project will succeed.

This is uncertainty.

But:

We do not agree on what “success” means.

This is ambiguity.

Uncertainty often requires evidence, probability, monitoring, or scenario thinking.

Ambiguity often requires definition, framing, negotiation, or conceptual clarification.

This distinction is essential.

A group may think it has an uncertainty problem when it actually has an ambiguity problem. People may debate forecasts when they have not agreed on the goal. They may ask whether a plan is “good” without defining good. They may argue about risk without defining acceptable risk.

Deliberate language asks:

Are we uncertain about the outcome, or unclear about the meaning?
Do we need more information, or do we need a better definition?
Are we missing evidence, or are we using unstable concepts?

For example:

We cannot evaluate the options yet because “success” is ambiguous. Before estimating risk, we need to define the target state.

This sentence prevents premature analysis.

8.14 Language for Provisional Judgment

Partial knowledge does not eliminate judgment. It makes judgment provisional.

A provisional judgment is a conclusion held under current evidence, open to revision if conditions or information change.

Useful language patterns include:

Based on current evidence, the best option appears to be…
This is our working judgment, not a final certainty.
This conclusion depends on the assumption that…
We should revise this judgment if…
The current evidence supports action at a limited scale.
We know enough to proceed with a test, but not enough to commit fully.

Provisional language is not weakness. It is disciplined confidence.

It allows action while keeping the mind responsive to reality.

A person who cannot make provisional judgments may wait forever.
A person who cannot revise provisional judgments may become trapped by early conclusions.

Deliberate language must support both movement and correction.

8.15 Bad Uncertainty Language

Bad uncertainty language either hides uncertainty or exaggerates it.

One form is overstatement:

This will definitely work.
There is no risk.
We fully understand the situation.

Another form is vague fear:

Something might go wrong.
This feels uncertain.
I do not know.
It is too complicated.

Another form is category confusion:

We know users want this.

when the real statement should be:

We assume users want this based on limited feedback.

Another form is false equality:

Since we are uncertain, all options are equally valid.

This is not true. Even under uncertainty, some options are better supported, more reversible, less costly, or more strategically aligned than others.

Another form is certainty laundering:

Everyone knows this will happen.

This phrase often hides weak evidence behind social pressure.

Deliberate language resists these failures by asking:

What do we actually know?
What evidence supports it?
What are we assuming?
What remains unclear?
How confident are we?
What would change our judgment?

These questions make uncertainty usable.

8.16 The Ethics of Saying “I Do Not Know”

“I do not know” is one of the most important sentences in deliberate language.

But it must be used precisely.

There is a responsible “I do not know”:

I do not know the answer yet, but I know what evidence would help.

There is also an evasive “I do not know”:

I do not know, so I will not think further.

The first opens inquiry.
The second closes responsibility.

A mature speaker can say:

I do not know.
I do not know yet.
I do not know enough to commit.
I do not know enough to reject.
I do not know, but I can test this.
I do not know, but the downside is small enough to explore.
I do not know, and the downside is too large to proceed blindly.

These distinctions matter. They turn ignorance into a structured position.

The goal is not to appear certain. The goal is to speak at the level of knowledge one actually has.

8.17 Chapter Summary

Uncertainty and partial knowledge are not obstacles outside deliberate language. They are central conditions that deliberate language must handle.

This chapter showed that partial knowledge is structured. A decision-maker must distinguish what is known, unknown, assumed, and unclear. He must mark degrees of confidence, protect the boundary between evidence and inference, and identify what remains unresolved.

Unknowns should not all be treated the same. Some require stopping. Some require research. Some require testing. Some require monitoring. Some can be accepted because the downside is limited or the action is reversible.

Good uncertainty language avoids both false certainty and vague doubt. It does not pretend to know everything, but it also does not surrender judgment merely because knowledge is incomplete.

Its core sentence is:

Based on what we know, under these assumptions, with these unknowns still present, this is the most responsible provisional judgment.

This is the foundation for the next chapter. Once uncertainty is represented clearly, the next task is to examine risk: what may happen, how likely it is, how serious it would be, and how language can prepare action under possible loss.
