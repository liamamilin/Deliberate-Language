3  The Situation of Speech

3.1 Speech as Situated Action

Language never happens in empty space. A sentence is spoken by someone, to someone, at a particular time, in a particular setting, within a particular relationship, and under particular stakes. This is why the same sentence can mean different things in different situations. The sentence “We need to talk” has a simple grammatical structure but its pragmatic meaning shifts dramatically depending on context: said by a friend after a misunderstanding it may signal repair, by a manager after a mistake it may signal evaluation, by a partner late at night it may signal emotional seriousness.

In earlier layers of language, the primary question may be what a sentence means. In pragmatic language, the question changes to what the speech is doing in the situation. A sentence may describe, request, warn, promise, refuse, comfort, pressure, accuse, invite, negotiate, clarify, command, or repair, and its function depends entirely on the situation in which it appears. The literal content is only one layer; the pragmatic function depends on who says it, to whom, when, where, under what relationship, and with what consequence. Language is therefore not merely a system of sentences; it is a system of situated moves.

This chapter studies the minimal structure of a speech event and asks what must be present for language to function as situated action. It provides a framework for understanding the six elements that constitute every speech event and offers practical guidance for designing speech that is appropriate to its situation.

3.2 The Six Elements of a Speech Event

A speech event can be understood as a structured interaction containing at least six elements: the speaker, the listener, timing, setting, relationship, and stakes. Each element shapes the meaning and force of what is said.

The speaker is the source of the speech act, bringing intention, position, knowledge, authority, emotion, responsibility, identity, and credibility. The same sentence changes meaning depending on who says it. “You should revise this” said by a teacher may be instruction, by a peer may be advice, by a client may be a requirement, by a stranger may feel intrusive. The speaker must ask: what role am I speaking from, do I have the authority to say this, and how will my identity affect interpretation?

The listener actively interprets, evaluates, accepts, resists, misunderstands, or responds. A sentence that is clear to one listener may be unclear to another. “Please handle this soon” may be sufficient for a close colleague but too vague for a new employee, a contractor, or a child. The listener determines what must be made explicit. A speaker must ask: what does the listener already know, what do they not know, what do they care about, and what misunderstanding is likely?

Timing changes meaning. The same words said before, during, and after an event do not have the same force. “Be careful” said before an action is a warning, during the action may be urgent guidance, and after an accident may sound like blame. A warning given too late becomes accusation; a clarification given too late becomes excuse; a decision given too late becomes abandonment. A competent speaker asks whether this is the right moment, whether action is still possible, and whether the listener is ready to hear what must be said.

Setting includes physical place, institutional context, medium, social norms, and surrounding conditions. A sentence spoken in private is not the same as in public; one spoken in a meeting is not the same as in a report; one sent in a casual chat is not the same as in a legal document. “This plan is weak” in a private review session may be direct feedback, while in a public meeting it may be humiliation. The speaker must ask whether the setting is public or private, formal or informal, synchronous or asynchronous, and what norms govern it.

Relationship shapes directness, politeness, permission, authority, emotional tone, and acceptable forms of disagreement. “You are wrong” may be acceptable between close collaborators with strong trust but may feel aggressive between strangers or disciplinary from a manager to an employee. The speaker must ask: what is my relationship to the listener, is there trust, is there power difference, and does this speech strengthen or threaten the relationship?

Stakes determine how carefully language must be designed. High-stakes speech usually requires precision, evidence, emotional control, explicit responsibility, clear next steps, and reduced ambiguity. Low-stakes speech can tolerate more looseness. The speaker must ask: what happens if I say this badly, what happens if I do not say this, who may be affected, and does this create obligation or change trust, safety, or future action?

These six elements form a diagnostic tool. When communication fails, one of these elements is often misread: the speaker may misjudge the listener, the timing may be poor, the setting may be inappropriate, the relationship may not support the level of directness, or the stakes may be higher than the speaker realizes.

3.3 Direct and Indirect Speech

The situation also shapes whether speech should be direct or indirect. Direct speech is useful when clarity, urgency, or accountability is required: “Please submit the report by five p.m. today.” Indirect speech is useful when politeness, relationship preservation, or emotional sensitivity matters: “It would be helpful if we could have the report before the end of the day.” Neither form is inherently better; the correct form depends on the situation.

High urgency often requires directness. Fragile relationships may require tact. High-stakes decisions require explicitness. Emotional repair may require softness. Operational execution requires clarity. A pragmatic speaker does not ask only whether the sentence is correct; the speaker asks whether the sentence is appropriate for this situation.

Speech has force. It can request, command, invite, warn, refuse, promise, apologize, evaluate, approve, reject, or authorize. But the force is not always visible from the words alone. “You may leave now” from a friend may sound strange, from a teacher may grant permission, from a judge may carry institutional authority, from a security officer may be an instruction. The force of speech depends on the social situation, and to design speech intentionally is to design its force.

Many communication failures are failures of situation reading rather than failures of vocabulary or grammar. The wrong listener is addressed when someone complains to a peer about a decision only leadership can change. The wrong timing occurs when detailed criticism is given during the first fragile stage of idea generation. The wrong setting occurs when someone is corrected harshly in front of a group. The wrong relationship calibration occurs when extreme directness is used with someone trust has not yet been built with. The wrong stakes assessment occurs when vague instructions are given for a task that affects safety, money, or legal responsibility.

3.4 Designing Speech for the Situation

A practical speech design process can begin with six questions corresponding to the six elements. First, who is speaking? Define role and authority: am I speaking as a friend, teacher, manager, parent, peer, expert, client, or learner? Second, who is listening? Define audience and capacity: what does the listener know, need, expect, fear, or misunderstand? Third, why now? Define timing: is this the right moment for warning, correction, request, decision, or repair? Fourth, where is this happening? Define setting: should this be said in public, private, writing, meeting, chat, or formal channel? Fifth, what is the relationship? Define social calibration: how much directness, explanation, politeness, or emotional care does this relationship require? Sixth, what is at stake? Define consequence: what happens if this speech succeeds, fails, or is misunderstood?

Consider feedback as an example. A situation-blind version might be “Your work is not good enough.” This lacks specificity, direction, and relationship care. A situation-aware version would consider all six elements. The speaker is a manager whose speech carries authority and may affect confidence. The listener needs concrete guidance, not merely judgment. The timing should be close enough to the event to be useful but not delivered in a moment of public embarrassment. The setting should be private. The tone should preserve respect while remaining honest. The stakes involve the employee’s performance, trust, and development. A stronger formulation would define the issue, the standard, the reason, the direction, the next action, and the deadline.

Consider a request as another example. “Help me with this” may be acceptable in a close relationship with shared context, but in many situations it is too vague. A situation-aware request defines what help is needed, why the listener is being asked, when it is needed, what level of effort is expected, and what outcome is desired. Good requests are designed to make cooperation possible, not merely polite.

Disagreement is one of the clearest tests of pragmatic competence. A situation-blind disagreement says “That is wrong.” A situation-aware disagreement might say “I see the direction, but I think one assumption needs to be tested. We are assuming users will change their workflow immediately, and I am not sure we have evidence for that.” This formulation preserves connection, identifies the specific disagreement, avoids attacking the person, moves discussion toward evidence, and keeps the decision process open.

3.5 The Speech Event Template and Diagnostic Checklist

A speech event can be analyzed using the following template: In [setting], [speaker] says [utterance] to [listener] at [timing], within [relationship], under [stakes], in order to [speech function]. For example: in a private review meeting, a manager tells a junior employee that the report needs revision after the first draft is submitted, within a developmental work relationship, under the stakes of client delivery and employee growth, in order to correct the work while preserving confidence and direction. This template makes the speech situation visible and helps distinguish sentence meaning from pragmatic function.

Before important speech, the following checklist should be used: who am I speaking as, who needs to hear this, why now, is this the right place or medium, what tone can this relationship carry, what happens if this is misunderstood, and what am I trying to do with this speech? This checklist is especially useful before giving criticism, making requests, setting boundaries, apologizing, refusing, negotiating, warning, making promises, and communicating decisions. In ordinary low-stakes speech, such analysis happens quickly and implicitly. In high-stakes speech, it should become explicit.

The more consequential the speech, the more responsibility the speaker carries for framing the situation correctly. Before speaking, one should consider who one is speaking as, who one is speaking to, why now, why here, what the relationship is, and what is at stake. These questions convert speech from impulse into deliberate action, especially in feedback, apology, negotiation, disagreement, instruction, refusal, warning, decision communication, and conflict repair. In such cases, language does not merely transmit content; it changes the social situation.

3.6 Chapter Summary

This chapter has argued that speech is situated action. A sentence cannot be understood by its words alone; its meaning and force depend on the speaker, the listener, timing, setting, relationship, and stakes. These six elements form the basic structure of every speech event, and communication fails when any of them is misread.

Direct and indirect speech each have their place depending on urgency, relationship, and stakes. The pragmatic speaker does not ask only whether a sentence is correct but whether it is appropriate for the situation. Situation-aware speech design requires answering six questions before speaking: who is speaking, who is listening, why now, where is this happening, what is the relationship, and what is at stake.

The responsibility for designing speech lies primarily with the speaker, especially in high-stakes situations where language changes the social landscape.

3.6.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied in every communicative act.

First, every speech event has six elements: speaker, listener, timing, setting, relationship, and stakes. Before any important utterance, check every one of them. Second, the same sentence changes meaning across different situations. You cannot judge a sentence by its words alone; you must read the situation in which it appears. Third, the speaker bears primary responsibility for designing speech that fits the situation, especially when stakes are high. Fourth, distinguish direct from indirect speech. Directness serves clarity and urgency; indirectness preserves relationship and emotional safety. Choose deliberately, not by habit. Fifth, before giving feedback, making a request, expressing disagreement, or communicating a difficult decision, run the six-question diagnostic: who am I speaking as, who needs to hear this, why now, is this the right setting, what tone can this relationship carry, and what is at stake if this is misunderstood. These five principles are the foundation of pragmatic competence. Internalize them until they become automatic.
