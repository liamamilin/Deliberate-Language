3  Goals, Criteria, and Stakes

3.1 From Problem to Purpose

A framed problem reveals what is wrong, what is unclear, or what must be changed. But it does not yet say what should be built, pursued, or achieved in its place. Between a problem and a plan lies a prior structure: the goal. The goal defines the direction, the criteria define the standards of judgment, and the stakes define what is at risk.

The previous chapter established problem framing as the first act of deliberate language. A problem frame identifies the object, boundary, condition, standard, cause, agent, consequence, and direction of a situation. This chapter moves one step forward: once a problem has been framed, the thinker must ask what success looks like, by what standards it will be judged, and what makes the situation matter enough to require deliberate attention. These questions prepare the ground for constructing options, which is the task of Chapter 3.

A weak goal says “I want things to improve.” A stronger goal says “I want to move from condition X to condition Y within Z constraints, and I will know I have succeeded when these specific criteria are met.” The second sentence contains structure: a current state, a desired state, a constraint, and a standard of verification. Without these elements, a goal remains an aspiration, not an object of deliberation.

3.2 Goals: Direction with Structure

A goal is a desired outcome that gives direction to thought and action. Goals are not merely wishes or fantasies; they carry structure, constraint, and commitment. A wish says “I would like to be successful.” A goal says “I aim to achieve X by Y, under Z conditions, because Q matters.” The difference is not merely linguistic precision; it is the difference between passive desire and deliberate intention.

Goals are constructed through language, not simply discovered. The act of stating a goal forces the thinker to decide what is wanted, what is prioritized, and what is excluded. A person who says “I want to improve my health” has not yet constructed a goal. He has named a domain of concern. A goal would specify: improve cardiovascular fitness to the point of being able to run five kilometers within three months, without injury, while maintaining current work schedule. This formulation specifies the target, the metric, the timeline, the constraint, and the boundary condition.

A goal serves several functions in deliberation. First, it provides direction: it tells the thinker which paths are aligned and which are irrelevant. Second, it provides criteria: from the goal, standards of evaluation can be derived. Third, it provides motivation: a goal that matters creates the energy to sustain deliberation through uncertainty and difficulty. Fourth, it provides closure: a goal defines what counts as completion, allowing the thinker to stop when it is achieved rather than pursuing endless improvement.

Goals can be classified along several dimensions. A goal may be specific or general, short-term or long-term, intrinsic or extrinsic, primary or secondary, concrete or abstract. Each type has its place, and the same decision may involve multiple goals at different levels. A career decision may involve a short-term goal of financial stability, a medium-term goal of skill building, and a long-term goal of meaningful contribution. Deliberate language must hold these levels in relation, not collapse them into one.

Not every strong desire is a good goal. A goal that is vague, contradictory, unmeasurable, unattainable within constraints, or misaligned with deeper values will produce confused judgment downstream. Deliberate language tests a goal by asking: can I describe the desired state concretely, can I recognize when it has been achieved, is it within my influence to pursue, what am I willing to sacrifice for it, does it conflict with other goals I hold, and does it serve the values that matter to me?

3.3 Criteria: From Goals to Standards

A criterion is a standard used to judge whether a goal has been met or which option best serves the goal. Criteria translate goals into evaluable terms. Without criteria, goals remain abstract and comparison becomes impossible.

The relationship between a goal and a criterion is one of derivation: the goal tells you what you want; the criterion tells you how to recognize it. If the goal is to improve spoken English fluency, the criteria may include: ability to sustain a five-minute conversation without significant hesitation, accuracy of pronunciation on commonly mispronounced sounds, and speed of word retrieval during spontaneous speech. These criteria do not exhaust the goal, but they make it operational.

A well-formed criterion has several properties. First, it is connected to the goal: a criterion that does not serve the goal will produce evaluation that is precise but irrelevant. Second, it is specific enough to be assessed: “good quality” is not a criterion; “defect rate below two percent” is. Third, it can be applied consistently across options: if the criterion changes meaning from one option to the next, comparison becomes impossible. Fourth, it is bounded by the context: a criterion appropriate for one decision may be irrelevant for another.

Criteria can be ranked by importance. Not every criterion carries equal weight; some are essential and some are desirable. A threshold criterion defines a minimum acceptable level: below this level, an option is excluded regardless of its performance on other dimensions. A ranking criterion differentiates among options that all meet the threshold. Distinguishing these two types prevents the thinker from treating all criteria as if they mattered equally.

Criteria also interact. Some criteria reinforce each other: improving speed may also reduce cost. Others conflict: increasing quality may increase cost. Deliberate language must surface these interactions before evaluation begins, so that the thinker is not surprised when a choice that optimizes one criterion weakens another.

The construction of criteria is itself an act of judgment. The thinker must decide which standards matter, where the thresholds lie, and how conflicts between criteria will be resolved when they appear. This work is best done before options are examined, because criteria constructed after seeing the options tend to be biased toward whichever option looks most appealing.

3.4 Stakes: What Makes It Matter

Stakes are what is at risk in a decision. They answer the question: why does this decision matter, for whom, and what is the cost of a wrong choice?

Stakes are not the same as criteria. Criteria define how success is measured; stakes define what is lost if success is not achieved. A decision may have clear criteria and low stakes (choosing a restaurant), or unclear criteria and high stakes (choosing a medical treatment). The level of stakes determines how much deliberation, evidence, and caution the decision deserves.

Stakes can be classified by type. The following table summarizes common categories of stakes.

Type Question Example
Material What resources are at risk? Money, time, assets
Relational What relationships are at risk? Trust, cooperation, reputation
Identity What sense of self is at risk? Values, self-image, integrity
Opportunity What future options are at risk? Career paths, learning, access
Emotional What psychological cost is at risk? Stress, regret, confidence
Moral What principles are at risk? Fairness, responsibility, harm

The same decision may carry multiple types of stakes simultaneously. Accepting a job offer involves material stakes (salary, benefits), relational stakes (new colleagues, leaving old team), identity stakes (what kind of professional you become), opportunity stakes (future career paths), emotional stakes (daily satisfaction), and potentially moral stakes (alignment with values). Deliberate language must name which types are present and which ones carry the most weight.

Stakes also have magnitude and distribution. A decision may have high stakes for one person and low stakes for another. In collective decisions, the stakes may be distributed unevenly: one party bears most of the cost while another party receives most of the benefit. Deliberate language must ask whose stakes are being considered and whether all affected parties have been included in the assessment.

The stakes of a decision affect the deliberation process itself. High-stakes decisions require more evidence, more careful framing, more explicit criteria, more thorough comparison, more risk analysis, and more contingency planning. Low-stakes decisions can tolerate shortcuts. The danger is misjudging the stakes: treating a high-stakes decision casually because the emotional discomfort of deliberation is avoided, or treating a low-stakes decision with excessive analysis because the thinker is avoiding action.

3.5 The Goal-Criteria-Stakes Triad

A goal without criteria cannot be evaluated. Criteria without stakes lack urgency. Stakes without a goal provide no direction. The three elements form a triad:

Goal — Direction
  ↓
Criteria — Standards of judgment
  ↓
Stakes — Consequence of failure or success

The goal sets the direction. The criteria translate the direction into assessable standards. The stakes explain why the direction matters enough to warrant deliberate thought. A decision that has all three is ready for option construction and comparison. A decision missing any one of them will produce distorted deliberation.

A practical template for testing the triad is: I aim to achieve [goal] because [stakes matter], and I will know I have succeeded when [criteria]. For example: “I aim to improve my spoken English fluency because my professional opportunities depend on confident communication, and I will know I have succeeded when I can sustain a five-minute spontaneous conversation in a professional context without frequent pauses or errors.” This sentence contains direction (improved fluency), stakes (professional opportunities), and criteria (five-minute conversation, spontaneous, professional context, without frequent pauses or errors). It is a complete starting point for constructing options and evaluating progress.

The triad is not static. As deliberation proceeds, goals may be clarified, criteria may be refined, and stakes may be reassessed. A goal that once seemed important may reveal itself as derivative of a deeper value; a criterion that seemed objective may reveal hidden assumptions; stakes that seemed personal may reveal collective consequences. The triad is maintained through iterative refinement, not fixed at the start and never revisited.

3.6 Chapter Summary

This chapter has studied goals, criteria, and stakes as the bridge between problem framing and option construction. A goal provides direction with structure, transforming vague desire into deliberate intention. Criteria translate goals into standards of judgment, making evaluation possible. Stakes define what is at risk, establishing why the decision matters.

The relationship between these elements is one of derivation: criteria derive from goals, and stakes derive from the values threatened or served by the decision. A well-formed decision triad connects all three: I aim at X, measured by Y, because Z matters.

The construction of criteria is itself an act of judgment. Threshold criteria define minimum acceptable levels; ranking criteria differentiate among acceptable options. Stakes may be material, relational, identity-based, opportunity-based, emotional, or moral, and the same decision may carry several types simultaneously.

3.6.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied whenever you clarify a goal, define a criterion, or assess stakes.

First, a goal is not a wish. A goal has structure: a desired state, a timeline, a constraint, and a standard of verification. If any of these is missing, the goal is not yet ready to guide judgment. Second, criteria are derived from goals. Every criterion must answer the question: how does this standard serve the goal? A criterion that does not connect to the goal is irrelevant, however precise it may be. Third, distinguish threshold criteria from ranking criteria. A threshold must be met; a ranking differentiates among options that meet the threshold. Confusing the two leads to either premature exclusion or indecisive comparison. Fourth, stakes are not the same as criteria. Criteria measure success; stakes measure what is lost if success is not achieved. A decision with high stakes demands more care, more evidence, and more contingency planning than a decision with low stakes. Fifth, construct criteria before examining options. Criteria built after seeing the options are vulnerable to confirmation bias. Sixth, test your goal-criteria-stakes triad before moving to option construction. A complete triad — I aim at X, measured by Y, because Z matters — provides the foundation for everything that follows, beginning with the construction of the decision space in Chapter 3.

3.6.2 🎯 行动指南

今日练习：为一个真实决策构建目标-准则-利害三元组

想一个你当前面临的实际决策（无论大小）。写下三句话：

目标：我想要从当前状态A达到状态B，在_时间/资源约束内。 准则：我知道自己成功当_、____、____（列出2-3个可检验的标准，区分阈值型和排名型）。 利害：这件事重要的因为____（列出至少一种利害类型：物质的、关系的、身份的、机会的、情绪的、道德的）。

测试你的三元组：如果你的目标和准则看起来合理，但你没有动力行动，检查利害——缺失的利害可能是真正的原因。如果准则之间有冲突，回到目标看看哪个准则更直接服务于目标。

行为调整提醒： - 下一次有人对你说”我想成功”或”我需要改善”时，反问：“成功长什么样？你怎么知道实现了？” - 做决定前先写准则，再看选项——这个顺序的反转（先看选项再定准则）是常见的认知偏差来源。 - 如果感觉一个决定”不对劲”，但准则都满足，检查有没有未命名的利害——通常是身份或道德层面的。
