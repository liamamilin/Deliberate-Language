3  The Situation of Speech

3.1 Language Happens Somewhere

Speech is never isolated.

A sentence does not appear in empty space.
It is spoken by someone, to someone, at a particular time, in a particular setting, within a particular relationship, under particular stakes.

This is why the same sentence can mean different things in different situations.

Consider the sentence:

“We need to talk.”

Its grammatical structure is simple.
Its pragmatic meaning is not.

Said by a friend after a misunderstanding, it may signal repair.
Said by a manager after a mistake, it may signal evaluation.
Said by a partner late at night, it may signal emotional seriousness.
Said by a teacher to a student, it may signal correction.
Said by a client to a contractor, it may signal dissatisfaction.

The words remain the same.

The situation changes the meaning.

This chapter studies the minimal structure of a speech event. It asks:

What must be present for language to function as situated action?

The answer is not only grammar, vocabulary, or sentence structure.

A speech event requires at least six elements:

Speaker
Listener
Timing
Setting
Relationship
Stakes

These elements form the basic situation of speech.

Without them, language cannot be fully understood.

3.2 Speech as Situated Action

In earlier layers of language, the primary question may be:

What does this sentence mean?

In pragmatic language, the question changes:

What is this speech doing in this situation?

A sentence may describe, request, warn, promise, refuse, comfort, pressure, accuse, invite, negotiate, clarify, command, or repair.

Its function depends on the situation in which it appears.

For example:

“It is cold here.”

As a description, it means the temperature is low.

But in real interaction, it may function as:

  • a complaint

  • a request to close the window

  • an indirect criticism

  • a hint

  • a shared observation

  • a test of whether the listener is attentive

The literal content is only one layer.

The pragmatic function depends on who says it, to whom, when, where, under what relationship, and with what consequence.

Language is therefore not merely a system of sentences.

It is a system of situated moves.

3.3 The Minimal Structure of a Speech Event

A speech event can be understood as a structured interaction.

At minimum, it contains the following elements:

Element Core Question Function
Speaker Who is speaking? Establishes source, intention, authority, responsibility
Listener Who is being addressed? Establishes audience, interpretation, obligation
Timing When is it said? Shapes urgency, appropriateness, and meaning
Setting Where and under what conditions is it said? Defines context and available norms
Relationship What is the relation between speaker and listener? Shapes power, distance, intimacy, permission
Stakes What matters if this speech succeeds or fails? Defines consequence and pressure

These elements are often invisible because competent speakers use them automatically.

But when they are ignored, communication breaks down.

A person may say the right words to the wrong listener.
Or say the right thing at the wrong time.
Or speak with the wrong degree of formality.
Or treat a high-stakes situation as casual.
Or use direct language in a relationship that requires tact.
Or use indirect language when clarity is required.

Pragmatic competence begins when language is understood inside its situation.

3.4 Speaker

Every speech event begins with a speaker.

But “speaker” does not only mean the person producing words.

The speaker is the source of the speech act.

The speaker brings:

  • intention

  • position

  • knowledge

  • authority

  • emotion

  • responsibility

  • identity

  • credibility

The same sentence changes depending on who says it.

Consider:

“You should revise this.”

Said by a teacher, it may be instruction.
Said by a peer, it may be advice.
Said by a client, it may be a requirement.
Said by a stranger, it may feel intrusive.
Said by an expert, it may carry authority.
Said by someone careless, it may be ignored.

The speaker’s position affects how the words are received.

A speaker must therefore ask:

  • What role am I speaking from?

  • Do I have the authority to say this?

  • Am I offering, requesting, instructing, judging, or warning?

  • What responsibility do I carry after saying this?

  • How will my identity affect interpretation?

Speech is never detached from the speaker’s position.

The speaker is part of the meaning.

3.5 Listener

Speech is addressed.

Even when someone appears to speak generally, the speech assumes an audience.

The listener is not a passive receiver.
The listener actively interprets, evaluates, accepts, resists, misunderstands, or responds.

A sentence that is clear to one listener may be unclear to another.

For example:

“Please handle this soon.”

To a close colleague who knows the project, this may be enough.
To a new employee, it may be too vague.
To a contractor, it may require a deadline.
To a child, it may require concrete steps.
To a legal or operational team, it may require written specification.

The listener determines what must be made explicit.

A speaker must therefore ask:

  • What does the listener already know?

  • What does the listener not know?

  • What does the listener care about?

  • What authority does the listener have?

  • What action can the listener take?

  • What misunderstanding is likely?

  • What emotional state might the listener be in?

Good pragmatic language is listener-aware.

It does not merely express what the speaker wants to say.
It shapes speech according to what the listener can understand, accept, and act on.

3.6 Timing

Timing changes meaning.

The same words said before an event, during an event, and after an event do not have the same force.

Consider:

“Be careful.”

Before the action, it is a warning.
During the action, it may be urgent guidance.
After the accident, it may sound like blame.

Timing affects:

  • urgency

  • appropriateness

  • emotional impact

  • perceived intention

  • available action

  • responsibility

Some speech acts must happen early.

A warning given too late becomes accusation.
A clarification given too late becomes excuse.
An apology given too late becomes damage control.
A decision given too late becomes abandonment.
A correction given too early may feel premature.

Timing is not external to language.

It is part of the speech act.

For example:

“I disagree.”

Said during planning, it may improve the decision.
Said after execution has already begun, it may disrupt coordination.
Said after failure, it may sound like self-protection.

A competent speaker asks:

  • Is this the right moment?

  • Is action still possible?

  • Is the listener ready to hear this?

  • Should this be said before, during, or after the event?

  • Will delay change the meaning?

  • Will early speech prevent later harm?

Timing gives language its practical force.

3.7 Setting

Speech occurs in a setting.

The setting includes physical place, institutional context, medium, social norms, and surrounding conditions.

A sentence spoken in private is not the same as a sentence spoken in public.

A sentence spoken in a meeting is not the same as a sentence written in a report.

A sentence sent in a casual chat is not the same as a sentence sent in a legal document.

Setting determines what kind of language is appropriate.

For example:

“This plan is weak.”

In a private review session, this may be direct feedback.
In a public meeting, it may be humiliation.
In a written evaluation, it may require evidence.
In a brainstorming session, it may suppress creativity.
In a crisis room, it may be necessary.

The setting changes the norms.

A speaker must ask:

  • Is this public or private?

  • Is this formal or informal?

  • Is this synchronous or asynchronous?

  • Is this written or spoken?

  • Is this casual, professional, legal, emotional, or operational?

  • What norms govern this setting?

  • What can be safely said here?

  • What should be moved to another setting?

A speech event does not only depend on people.
It depends on the environment in which speech appears.

3.8 Relationship

Speech is shaped by relationship.

Language between strangers differs from language between close friends.
Language between peers differs from language between manager and employee.
Language between parent and child differs from language between client and vendor.

Relationship affects:

  • directness

  • politeness

  • permission

  • authority

  • emotional tone

  • risk of offense

  • degree of explanation required

  • acceptable forms of disagreement

Consider:

“You are wrong.”

Between close collaborators with strong trust, this may be acceptable.
Between strangers, it may be aggressive.
From a manager to an employee, it may feel disciplinary.
From a junior employee to a senior leader, it may require careful framing.
In an academic debate, it may be normal if supported by evidence.

The relationship determines how language should be calibrated.

A speaker must ask:

  • What is my relationship to the listener?

  • Is there trust?

  • Is there power difference?

  • Is directness permitted?

  • Is the relationship fragile or stable?

  • Does this speech strengthen or threaten the relationship?

  • Do I need to preserve dignity while delivering content?

Pragmatic language is not only about saying what is true.

It is about saying what is true in a way that the relationship can carry.

3.9 Stakes

Every speech event has stakes.

Sometimes the stakes are low:

“Where should we eat?”

Sometimes the stakes are high:

“This treatment may not work.”
“We need to cut the project.”
“Your performance is below expectation.”
“I cannot agree to this contract.”
“The system may fail if we continue.”

Stakes determine how carefully language must be designed.

High-stakes speech usually requires:

  • precision

  • evidence

  • emotional control

  • explicit responsibility

  • clear next steps

  • reduced ambiguity

  • awareness of consequences

Low-stakes speech can tolerate more looseness.

A speaker must ask:

  • What happens if I say this badly?

  • What happens if I do not say this?

  • Who may be affected?

  • Is this reversible or irreversible?

  • Does this create obligation?

  • Does this change trust, money, safety, status, or future action?

Stakes transform language from casual expression into responsible action.

The higher the stakes, the less language can rely on implication alone.

3.10 The Six-Part Situation Model

The situation of speech can be represented as a minimal model:

Speaker → Listener
   |          |
Timing — Setting
   |          |
Relationship — Stakes

This model is not a rigid formula.

It is a diagnostic tool.

When communication fails, one of these elements is often misread.

The speaker may misjudge the listener.
The listener may misread the speaker’s intention.
The timing may be poor.
The setting may be inappropriate.
The relationship may not support the level of directness.
The stakes may be higher than the speaker realizes.

A speech event is successful when these elements are aligned.

It fails when one or more of them are mismatched.

3.11 Example: Same Sentence, Different Situations

Consider the sentence:

“Can you finish this today?”

Its meaning depends on the situation.

3.11.1 Manager to Employee

The sentence may function as a task assignment.

The employee may hear:

“This is expected.”

The relationship contains authority.
The stakes may involve performance.

3.11.2 Peer to Peer

The sentence may function as coordination.

The listener may hear:

“Can I rely on you?”

The relationship is more horizontal.
The stakes involve mutual dependency.

3.11.3 Client to Contractor

The sentence may function as pressure.

The listener may hear:

“Delivery is being demanded.”

The setting may imply contractual expectation.

3.11.4 Friend to Friend

The sentence may function as a favor request.

The listener may hear:

“I need help.”

The relationship may soften the pressure.

3.11.5 During a Crisis

The sentence may function as urgent command.

The listener may hear:

“This must happen immediately.”

Timing and stakes raise the force of the sentence.

The sentence is grammatically identical in all cases.

But pragmatically, it is different each time.

3.12 When the Situation Is Misread

Many communication failures are not failures of vocabulary or grammar.

They are failures of situation reading.

3.12.1 Wrong Listener

The speaker addresses someone who cannot act.

Example:

Complaining to a peer about a decision only leadership can change.

The speech may express frustration, but it does not reach the responsible agent.

3.12.2 Wrong Timing

The speaker says something too early or too late.

Example:

Giving detailed criticism during the first fragile stage of idea generation.

The content may be valid, but the timing damages the process.

3.12.3 Wrong Setting

The speaker uses a public setting for speech that requires privacy.

Example:

Correcting someone harshly in front of a group.

The issue may be real, but the setting adds humiliation.

3.12.4 Wrong Relationship Calibration

The speaker uses a tone that the relationship cannot support.

Example:

Speaking with extreme directness to someone with whom trust has not been built.

The message may be accurate, but the relationship cannot carry it.

3.12.5 Wrong Stakes Assessment

The speaker treats a high-stakes issue casually.

Example:

Giving vague instructions for a task that affects safety, money, or legal responsibility.

The language is too loose for the consequence.

These failures show that effective speech requires situational intelligence.

3.13 Speech Situation and Responsibility

The more consequential the speech, the more responsibility the speaker carries for framing the situation correctly.

This does not mean the speaker controls all interpretation.

Listeners also carry responsibility.
They must listen carefully, ask clarifying questions, and avoid careless interpretation.

But the speaker has first responsibility for designing the speech act.

Before speaking, one should ask:

Who am I speaking as?
Who am I speaking to?
Why now?
Why here?
What is our relationship?
What is at stake?

These questions convert speech from impulse into deliberate action.

They are especially important in:

  • feedback

  • apology

  • negotiation

  • disagreement

  • instruction

  • refusal

  • warning

  • decision communication

  • conflict repair

In such cases, language does not merely transmit content.

It changes the social situation.

3.14 Direct and Indirect Speech

The situation also shapes whether speech should be direct or indirect.

Direct speech is useful when clarity, urgency, or accountability is required.

Example:

“Please submit the report by 5 p.m. today.”

Indirect speech is useful when politeness, relationship preservation, or emotional sensitivity matters.

Example:

“It would be helpful if we could have the report before the end of the day.”

Neither directness nor indirectness is inherently better.

The correct form depends on the situation.

High urgency often requires directness.
Fragile relationships may require tact.
High-stakes decisions require explicitness.
Emotional repair may require softness.
Operational execution requires clarity.

A pragmatic speaker does not ask only:

“Is this sentence correct?”

The speaker asks:

“Is this sentence appropriate for this situation?”

3.15 The Situation Determines the Force of Speech

Speech has force.

It can request, command, invite, warn, refuse, promise, apologize, evaluate, approve, reject, or authorize.

But the force is not always visible from the words alone.

Consider:

“You may leave now.”

From a friend, it may sound strange.
From a teacher, it may grant permission.
From a judge, it may carry institutional authority.
From a security officer, it may be an instruction.
From a host, it may be rude depending on tone and setting.

The force of speech depends on the social situation.

This is why pragmatic language cannot be reduced to sentence meaning.

The same sentence may carry different force because the speaker occupies a different role, the listener has a different obligation, or the setting gives the words institutional weight.

To understand speech, one must understand its force.

To design speech, one must design its force intentionally.

3.16 Situation-Aware Speech Design

A practical speech design process can begin with six questions.

3.16.1 Who is speaking?

Define role and authority.

Am I speaking as a friend, teacher, manager, parent, peer, expert, client, citizen, or learner?

3.16.2 Who is listening?

Define audience and capacity.

What does the listener know, need, expect, fear, or misunderstand?

3.16.3 Why now?

Define timing.

Is this the right moment for warning, correction, request, decision, or repair?

3.16.4 Where is this happening?

Define setting.

Should this be said in public, private, writing, meeting, chat, document, or formal channel?

3.16.5 What is the relationship?

Define social calibration.

How much directness, explanation, politeness, or emotional care does this relationship require?

3.16.6 What is at stake?

Define consequence.

What happens if this speech succeeds, fails, or is misunderstood?

These questions form the basic discipline of pragmatic speech.

They prevent language from being treated as disembodied text.

3.17 Example: Giving Feedback

Suppose a manager needs to give feedback to an employee.

A situation-blind version might be:

“Your work is not good enough.”

This sentence may be clear, but it is pragmatically weak.

It lacks specificity, direction, and relationship care.

A situation-aware version would consider the six elements.

3.17.1 Speaker

The speaker is a manager.
The speech carries authority and may affect confidence, evaluation, and future behavior.

3.17.2 Listener

The listener may need concrete guidance, not merely judgment.

3.17.3 Timing

Feedback should be close enough to the event to be useful, but not delivered in a moment of public embarrassment.

3.17.4 Setting

Private setting is usually better than public correction.

3.17.5 Relationship

The tone should preserve respect while still being honest.

3.17.6 Stakes

The employee’s performance, trust, and development are affected.

A stronger formulation:

“The current draft does not yet meet the standard we need for client delivery. The main issue is not effort, but structure: the argument is difficult to follow, and the evidence is not connected clearly to the recommendation. Please revise the draft by reorganizing it around three claims, adding supporting evidence under each one, and sending me the revised version by tomorrow afternoon.”

This speech is more effective because it defines:

  • the issue

  • the standard

  • the reason

  • the direction

  • the next action

  • the deadline

It is not merely more polite.

It is more structurally responsible.

3.18 Example: Making a Request

Consider a request:

“Help me with this.”

This may be acceptable in a close relationship with shared context.

But in many situations, it is too vague.

A situation-aware request defines:

  • what help is needed

  • why the listener is being asked

  • when it is needed

  • what level of effort is expected

  • what outcome is desired

For example:

“Could you review the introduction section today and tell me whether the problem statement is clear? I do not need line editing yet; I only need feedback on structure.”

This version reduces misunderstanding.

It respects the listener’s time.
It defines the task.
It names the boundary.
It clarifies the expected response.

Good requests are not merely polite.

They are designed to make cooperation possible.

3.19 Example: Disagreement

Disagreement is one of the clearest tests of pragmatic competence.

A situation-blind disagreement says:

“That is wrong.”

Sometimes this is acceptable. Often it is not enough.

A situation-aware disagreement may say:

“I see the direction, but I think one assumption needs to be tested. We are assuming users will change their workflow immediately, and I am not sure we have evidence for that.”

This formulation does several things:

  • preserves connection

  • identifies the specific disagreement

  • avoids attacking the person

  • moves the discussion toward evidence

  • keeps the decision process open

In high-trust technical debate, more direct language may be acceptable:

“I disagree with the architecture choice because it creates a single point of failure.”

Here, directness is useful because the issue is concrete and the stakes may be high.

The question is not whether disagreement should be direct or indirect.

The question is:

What form of disagreement fits this situation?

3.20 Speech Event Template

A speech event can be analyzed using the following template:

In [setting], [speaker] says [utterance] to [listener]
at [timing],
within [relationship],
under [stakes],
in order to [speech function].

Example:

In a private review meeting, a manager tells a junior employee that the report needs revision
after the first draft is submitted,
within a developmental work relationship,
under the stakes of client delivery and employee growth,
in order to correct the work while preserving confidence and direction.

This template makes the speech situation visible.

It helps distinguish sentence meaning from pragmatic function.

3.21 Minimal Diagnostic Checklist

Before important speech, use the following checklist:

Speaker: Who am I speaking as?
Listener: Who needs to hear this?
Timing: Why now?
Setting: Is this the right place or medium?
Relationship: What tone can this relationship carry?
Stakes: What happens if this is misunderstood?
Function: What am I trying to do with this speech?

This checklist is especially useful before:

  • giving criticism

  • making requests

  • setting boundaries

  • apologizing

  • refusing

  • negotiating

  • warning

  • making promises

  • communicating decisions

In ordinary low-stakes speech, such analysis happens quickly and implicitly.

In high-stakes speech, it should become explicit.

3.22 Chapter Summary

Speech is not only made of words.

It is made of words situated within a human event.

The minimal structure of a speech event includes:

Speaker
Listener
Timing
Setting
Relationship
Stakes

These elements shape what speech means, what force it carries, and what consequences it produces.

The same sentence may function differently depending on who says it, who hears it, when it is said, where it is said, what relationship exists, and what is at stake.

Pragmatic language begins when speech is no longer treated as isolated expression, but as situated action.

To speak well is not only to form correct sentences.

It is to understand the situation in which those sentences must act.
