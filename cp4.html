5  Informing, Asking, and Directing

5.1 Three Basic Movements of Speech

Speech often begins with one of three basic movements:

Informing
Asking
Directing

These are among the most common operations in pragmatic language.

To inform is to give information.
To ask is to seek information.
To direct is to move someone toward action.

At the sentence level, these may appear simple.

Informing often uses statements.
Asking often uses questions.
Directing often uses commands or requests.

But pragmatic language is not controlled by sentence form alone.

A statement can function as a direction.
A question can function as criticism.
A command can function as care.
A piece of information can function as warning, pressure, invitation, or refusal.

For example:

“The report is due tomorrow.”

This may simply inform the listener of a deadline.

But in another situation, it may mean:

“You need to finish it now.”

The form is informational.
The function may be directive.

This chapter examines the difference between giving information, seeking information, and moving others toward action.

The central question is:

What is the speech trying to change?

If it tries to change what the listener knows, it is informing.
If it tries to obtain what the speaker does not know, it is asking.
If it tries to change what the listener does, it is directing.

5.2 Informing

Informing is the act of giving information to another person.

Its basic structure is:

Speaker → Information → Listener

The speaker changes the listener’s knowledge state.

Examples:

“The meeting starts at 3 p.m.”
“The file has been updated.”
“The client rejected the proposal.”
“The road is closed.”
“Your application has been approved.”

These utterances do not directly request action.

They provide information.

However, information often prepares action.

If someone says:

“The road is closed.”

The listener may infer:

“We need another route.”

If someone says:

“The client rejected the proposal.”

The listener may infer:

“We need to revise it.”

Information changes what action becomes reasonable.

This is why informing is not passive.

To inform is to alter the listener’s map of reality.

5.3 The Responsibility of Informing

Informing carries responsibility because information affects judgment and action.

A speaker who informs must consider:

  • accuracy

  • completeness

  • relevance

  • timing

  • source

  • uncertainty

  • consequence

Bad informing can mislead even without direct lying.

For example:

“The project is fine.”

This may be technically true if no visible crisis has occurred. But if the speaker knows there are serious hidden risks, the statement may be pragmatically misleading.

A more responsible formulation would be:

“The project is still on schedule, but two dependencies are unresolved and may affect delivery next week.”

This statement separates:

  • current status

  • uncertainty

  • risk

  • time horizon

Good informing does not merely state a fact.

It gives the listener enough structure to understand what the fact means.

5.4 Complete and Incomplete Information

Not all information is equally useful.

A weak informational statement may be true but insufficient.

Example:

“There is a problem with the system.”

This informs, but poorly.

The listener still does not know:

  • what system

  • what kind of problem

  • how serious it is

  • who is affected

  • whether action is required

  • how urgent it is

A stronger version:

“The payment system is rejecting card transactions from new users. Existing users are not affected. The issue started at 10:20 a.m., and engineering is investigating. We need a status update within one hour.”

This gives:

  • object

  • issue

  • scope

  • timing

  • affected group

  • current action

  • next expectation

Informing becomes stronger when it answers the listener’s likely needs.

A useful information act usually clarifies:

What happened?
Where did it happen?
Who is affected?
When did it happen?
How certain are we?
Why does it matter?
What happens next?

Not every situation requires all of these. But high-stakes informing usually requires more structure.

5.5 Informing Without Directing

A key distinction:

Informing gives the listener knowledge.
Directing asks the listener to act.

These can be separated.

For example:

“The deadline is Friday.”

This informs.

“Please submit the draft by Friday.”

This directs.

The first changes awareness.
The second creates an expectation of action.

Confusion occurs when speakers inform but expect action.

For example:

“The trash is full.”

The speaker may intend:

“Please take out the trash.”

But the listener may treat it as information.

This can create frustration.

The speaker thinks the meaning was obvious.
The listener hears only a statement.

In low-stakes familiar relationships, indirect informing may work.

In high-stakes or operational situations, direct action language is usually better.

If action is required, say the action.

5.6 Asking

Asking is the act of seeking information, confirmation, permission, judgment, or action from the listener.

Its basic structure is:

Speaker → Information Gap → Listener

The speaker presents a gap and invites the listener to fill it.

Examples:

“What time does the meeting start?”
“Did you send the file?”
“Why did the system fail?”
“Can we delay the launch?”
“What do you think of this plan?”

Asking is not only grammatical questioning.

It is a social act.

When a speaker asks, the listener is placed in a position of response.

This creates a small obligation.

The listener may answer, refuse, delay, clarify, redirect, or challenge the question.

Questions therefore shape interaction.

They decide what kind of information becomes relevant.

5.7 Types of Asking

Not all questions seek the same kind of response.

Type Function Example
Factual Question Seeks information “When did this happen?”
Clarifying Question Reduces ambiguity “Do you mean the first version or the final version?”
Diagnostic Question Searches for cause “What changed before the error appeared?”
Evaluative Question Seeks judgment “Is this good enough for release?”
Permission Question Seeks authorization “Can I publish this?”
Preference Question Seeks desire or choice “Which option do you prefer?”
Challenge Question Tests reasoning “What evidence supports that conclusion?”
Reflective Question Prompts thinking “What are we assuming here?”
Rhetorical Question Does not expect a real answer “How could this possibly work?”

A good question makes clear what kind of answer is needed.

A vague question burdens the listener.

For example:

“Thoughts?”

This may be acceptable in casual conversation, but in many situations it is too open.

Better:

“Do you think the argument is clear enough for a non-technical reader?”

or:

“Can you check whether the second section supports the main claim?”

The better question defines the dimension of response.

5.8 Asking as Attention Control

A question directs attention.

It tells the listener where to look.

Compare:

“Why did this fail?”

with:

“What condition made failure likely?”

The first may invite blame.
The second invites structural analysis.

Compare:

“Who is responsible?”

with:

“Which part of the process lacked ownership?”

The first searches for a person.
The second searches for a responsibility gap.

Compare:

“Do you like this?”

with:

“Does this solve the user’s problem?”

The first invites preference.
The second invites evaluation under criteria.

Questions are not neutral.

They shape the space of thought.

A strong question does not merely request information.
It creates a direction for inquiry.

5.9 The Responsibility of Asking

Asking also carries responsibility.

A question can clarify, but it can also confuse, pressure, accuse, manipulate, or narrow thought.

For example:

“Why are you always late?”

This is formally a question, but pragmatically it contains accusation and generalization.

A better question:

“What is making it difficult to arrive by 9 a.m. consistently?”

This version still addresses the issue, but it changes the frame.

Another example:

“Don’t you think this plan is unrealistic?”

This question pushes the listener toward agreement.

A more open version:

“What risks do you see in this plan?”

Asking responsibly means designing questions that match the purpose of the conversation.

If the goal is inquiry, the question should keep inquiry open.
If the goal is decision, the question should focus criteria.
If the goal is accountability, the question should be specific.
If the goal is repair, the question should reduce defensiveness.

Bad questions often create bad thinking.

Good questions make better thinking possible.

5.10 Asking Versus Requesting

A question can ask for information.

But it can also request action.

Consider:

“Can you send me the file?”

Literally, this asks about ability.

Pragmatically, it requests action.

The listener normally understands:

“Please send me the file.”

This shows that asking and directing often overlap.

A question form may soften a directive.

Compare:

“Send me the file.”

with:

“Can you send me the file?”

The second is less direct and more polite.

But the function is still directive.

This distinction matters because people often hide directions inside questions.

For example:

“Can you be more careful next time?”

This may be a request, criticism, warning, or instruction.

If the stakes are low, indirectness may be fine.

If the stakes are high, the speaker should make the action explicit:

“Next time, please verify the numbers against the source file before submitting the report.”

This reduces ambiguity.

5.11 Directing

Directing is the act of moving someone toward action.

Its basic structure is:

Speaker → Desired Action → Listener

Examples:

“Please close the door.”
“Submit the report by 5 p.m.”
“Call the client before noon.”
“Do not publish this yet.”
“Use the revised version.”
“Focus on the first problem.”

Directing does not always appear as a command.

It may appear as:

  • request

  • suggestion

  • instruction

  • warning

  • recommendation

  • invitation

  • assignment

  • prohibition

  • reminder

For example:

“You might want to check the numbers again.”

This may function as gentle direction.

“The numbers need to be checked before this goes out.”

This directs through necessity.

“Do not send this until the numbers are checked.”

This directs explicitly.

Different forms carry different levels of force.

5.12 Degrees of Directive Force

Directing can be soft or strong.

Form Example Force
Suggestion “You might consider revising this.” Low
Advice “I think you should revise this.” Moderate
Request “Please revise this.” Moderate
Instruction “Revise this before submitting.” Strong
Command “Revise this now.” Very strong
Prohibition “Do not submit this version.” Strong
Requirement “This must be revised before approval.” Very strong

The right degree depends on:

  • authority

  • urgency

  • relationship

  • stakes

  • listener competence

  • need for autonomy

  • risk of misunderstanding

Too much force can create resistance.

Too little force can create confusion.

For example, in a safety-critical situation:

“Maybe we should consider stopping the machine.”

This may be too weak.

Better:

“Stop the machine now.”

In a creative discussion:

“Change this immediately.”

may be too strong.

Better:

“This section may need a different structure. Can we explore another version?”

Directive force must fit the situation.

5.13 The Structure of a Good Direction

A good direction usually includes:

Action
Object
Standard
Timing
Reason
Owner

Example:

“Please revise the introduction by making the problem statement more specific, and send the updated version by tomorrow afternoon so we can review it before the client meeting.”

This includes:

  • action: revise

  • object: introduction

  • standard: more specific problem statement

  • timing: tomorrow afternoon

  • reason: review before client meeting

  • owner: listener

A weak direction:

“Make it better.”

This is too vague.

The listener does not know:

  • what to change

  • where to change it

  • by when

  • according to what standard

  • for what purpose

Good directing reduces unnecessary interpretation.

It does not force the listener to guess the speaker’s intention.

5.14 Direction and Authority

Directing always raises the question of authority.

Who has the right to direct whom?

Authority may come from:

  • role

  • expertise

  • ownership

  • responsibility

  • agreement

  • urgency

  • emergency

  • relationship

  • institutional position

A manager may direct an employee because of organizational role.

A doctor may direct a patient because of expertise.

A team member may direct another in a crisis because immediate coordination is needed.

A friend may make a request, but not command.

When authority is unclear, directive language can create tension.

For example:

“Do this by tomorrow.”

From a manager, it may be normal.

From a peer, it may sound controlling.

From a client, it may depend on contract and relationship.

From a stranger, it may be unacceptable.

Good directive speech respects the authority structure of the situation.

When authority is weak or shared, directions often need softer forms:

Could you take this part?
Would it make sense for you to handle the data check?
Can we agree that you will send the first draft by Friday?
I suggest we pause here and verify the assumption first.

The weaker the authority, the more the speaker must rely on agreement, reason, or negotiation.

5.15 Directing Without Clarity

Many coordination failures happen because directions are unclear.

A speaker may think they have directed, when they have only hinted.

For example:

“This needs attention.”

This may mean:

  • fix it now

  • review it later

  • assign someone

  • monitor it

  • report back

  • ignore it unless it worsens

The phrase does not define the action.

A stronger version:

“Please review this section today, identify the source of the error, and tell me by 4 p.m. whether it can be fixed before release.”

This direction specifies action, object, deadline, and expected output.

Another vague direction:

“Be more proactive.”

Better:

“When you notice a blocker, please post it in the project channel the same day and propose one possible next step.”

The second version turns an abstract behavioral expectation into observable action.

5.16 Informing, Asking, and Directing Compared

The three speech actions can be compared by what they attempt to change.

Speech Action What It Changes Basic Form Listener Burden
Informing Listener’s knowledge “X is the case.” Understand and update
Asking Speaker’s knowledge or shared understanding “What is X?” Answer, clarify, or respond
Directing Listener’s action “Do X.” Act, refuse, negotiate, or comply

The difference is not always grammatical.

A statement may direct.
A question may request.
A direction may inform.

The real test is function:

Is the speaker giving information?
Is the speaker seeking information?
Is the speaker moving the listener toward action?

5.17 Mixed Speech Acts

Many utterances combine informing, asking, and directing.

Example:

“The client meeting is tomorrow. Can you revise the summary tonight?”

This informs and directs through a question.

Example:

“The numbers look inconsistent. Could you check the source file?”

This informs and requests action.

Example:

“What changed in the pipeline, and please document the fix afterward.”

This asks and directs.

Mixed speech acts are common because real situations require multiple moves.

But when the stakes are high, the speaker should separate the functions clearly.

For example:

Information: The client found three errors in the report.
Question: Do we know where the errors came from?
Direction: Please trace the source, correct the numbers, and send me an update by 3 p.m.

Separating functions reduces confusion.

5.18 Indirect Direction

People often direct indirectly.

They may say:

“It is noisy here.”

instead of:

“Please lower your voice.”

They may say:

“The deadline is close.”

instead of:

“You need to finish this today.”

They may say:

“This section is unclear.”

instead of:

“Rewrite this section.”

Indirect direction can be useful.

It may protect politeness, reduce pressure, or allow the listener to act voluntarily.

But indirect direction also risks misunderstanding.

A listener may not recognize that action is expected.

The speaker may then become frustrated:

“I already told you.”

But the speaker did not direct clearly.

A useful principle:

If action is required, make the action visible.

The more important the action, the less it should depend on inference.

5.19 Refusal as Response to Direction

Directing creates the possibility of refusal.

A listener may say:

“I cannot do that.”
“Not by today.”
“I can do part of it.”
“I need more information first.”
“That is not my responsibility.”
“I disagree with that action.”

This does not necessarily mean the direction failed.

It may mean the direction entered negotiation.

A good directive act should allow reality to respond.

For example:

“Can you finish this by 5 p.m.?”

The listener may answer:

“I can finish the draft by 5, but not the final version.”

This response clarifies capacity.

Directive speech becomes stronger when it allows constraints to surface.

A bad direction demands compliance without checking feasibility.

A good direction creates action while still allowing clarification.

5.20 Asking Before Directing

In uncertain situations, asking should often come before directing.

If the speaker lacks enough information, immediate direction may be premature.

For example:

“Fix the login bug now.”

This may be necessary in a crisis.

But if the situation is unclear, better:

“What do we know about the login bug? Who is affected? Is there a workaround? What is the fastest safe fix?”

After asking, direction can become more accurate:

“Given that only new users are affected and the workaround is available, prioritize diagnosis first and update support with the workaround message.”

Good leadership often moves through this sequence:

Ask → Understand → Direct

Bad leadership often skips the first two steps:

Assume → Direct

The result is misdirected action.

5.21 Informing Before Asking

Sometimes informing should come before asking.

If the listener lacks context, a question may be confusing.

Weak version:

“What should we do?”

Stronger version:

“The launch is scheduled for Friday, but testing found two unresolved payment errors. Engineering says one can be fixed today, but the second may take three days. What should we do about the launch date?”

The second version informs before asking.

It gives the listener enough background to answer responsibly.

Good questions often require good informing first.

Without context, asking becomes a burden.

5.22 Directing After Informing

Sometimes direction should follow information.

Weak version:

“Submit the revised proposal today.”

Stronger version:

“The client moved the review meeting to tomorrow morning, so we need the revised proposal today. Please update the pricing section and send it by 6 p.m.”

The information explains the direction.

This matters because reasons improve cooperation.

People are more likely to act well when they understand why the action matters.

A direction without reason may produce compliance.

A direction with reason can produce coordination.

5.23 Failure Modes

5.23.1 Mistaking Information for Direction

Speaker:

“The room is messy.”

Intended meaning:

“Clean the room.”

Listener hears:

“The room is messy.”

The speaker expected action but gave information.

5.23.2 Asking a Question When Giving a Direction

Speaker:

“Can you try to be on time?”

Intended meaning:

“You must arrive on time.”

Listener hears:

“I should try, but it is flexible.”

The speaker used a weak question where a clear expectation was needed.

5.23.3 Directing Without Information

Speaker:

“Change the design.”

Listener asks:

“Why? Which part? According to what standard?”

The direction lacks context.

5.23.4 Informing Without Relevance

Speaker:

“There are many issues.”

Listener asks:

“Which issues matter? What should I do?”

The information is too general to support action.

5.23.5 Asking Without Scope

Speaker:

“What do you think?”

Listener asks:

“About what dimension?”

The question is too open.

5.23.6 Over-Directing

Speaker:

“Do exactly this.”

But the listener has more local knowledge.

Over-direction can suppress judgment and reduce adaptation.

5.23.7 Under-Directing

Speaker:

“Maybe we should look at this sometime.”

But action is urgent.

Under-direction can create delay and ambiguity.

5.24 Practical Templates

5.24.1 Informing Template

The current situation is [fact/status].
This matters because [relevance/stakes].
The uncertainty is [unknown/condition], if any.
The next update/action is [next step], if needed.

Example:

The current situation is that the payment error affects new users only.
This matters because signups are dropping during onboarding.
The cause is still uncertain.
The next update will come after engineering checks the API logs.

5.24.2 Asking Template

I need to understand [information gap].
Specifically, can you clarify [focused question]?
This matters because [decision/action depending on answer].

Example:

I need to understand where the delay is coming from.
Specifically, can you clarify whether the blocker is design approval or backend implementation?
This matters because we need to decide whether to change scope or add support.

5.24.3 Directing Template

Please [action] on [object] by [time],
using [standard/method],
because [reason/stakes].
Tell me [expected response/output] when done.

Example:

Please revise the first section by tomorrow afternoon,
using the problem-goal-options structure,
because we need the chapter to align with the rest of the volume.
Tell me when the revised version is ready for review.

5.25 Minimal Operational Distinction

In real speech, a simple diagnostic is useful:

Informing: I want you to know X.
Asking: I want to know X from you.
Directing: I want you to do X.

This distinction prevents many communication failures.

Before speaking, ask:

Am I trying to inform, ask, or direct?

If the goal is knowledge transfer, inform clearly.
If the goal is information retrieval, ask precisely.
If the goal is action, direct responsibly.

Do not hide action inside vague information when clarity is needed.

Do not use questions to disguise commands when responsibility should be explicit.

Do not direct before asking when the situation is uncertain.

5.26 Chapter Summary

Informing, asking, and directing are three basic speech actions.

Informing changes what the listener knows.
Asking seeks information, judgment, permission, or clarification.
Directing moves the listener toward action.

These functions often overlap, but they should not be confused.

A statement may function as direction.
A question may function as request.
A direction may require information to make sense.

Pragmatic competence requires the speaker to understand what kind of change the utterance is meant to produce.

The central discipline is simple:

Know whether your speech is giving information, seeking information, or creating action.

When this distinction is clear, communication becomes more stable.

When it is unclear, people may know something but not act, be asked something but not understand the question, or receive a direction without knowing what to do.

Language becomes effective when its function matches its form, context, and intended consequence.
