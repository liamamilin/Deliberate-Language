7  Chapter 6 — Positioning Through Language

7.1 Language Places People

Language does not only carry meaning.

It places people.

When someone speaks, they are not only saying something about the world. They are also positioning themselves and others within a social field.

A person may speak as an expert, a beginner, a friend, a manager, a subordinate, a witness, a critic, a supporter, a negotiator, or an outsider.

The content of speech matters.

But the stance of speech also matters.

Consider the difference between:

“You need to change this.”

and:

“I wonder if this part could be revised.”

Both may point toward the same issue.

But they position the speaker differently.

The first places the speaker in a position of authority.
The second places the speaker in a position of suggestion or collaboration.

The listener is also positioned differently.

In the first sentence, the listener is someone expected to comply.
In the second sentence, the listener is someone invited to consider.

This chapter examines how language establishes:

Authority
Distance
Alignment
Confidence
Relational stance

These are not decorative features of speech.

They shape how language is received, resisted, accepted, or acted upon.

7.2 Positioning as a Pragmatic Function

Positioning means using language to establish where one stands in relation to others.

It answers questions such as:

  • Am I above, below, or beside the listener?

  • Am I close to or distant from the listener?

  • Am I aligned with or opposed to the listener?

  • Am I certain, uncertain, tentative, or assertive?

  • Am I speaking as a helper, judge, expert, peer, leader, or outsider?

  • Am I inviting, instructing, challenging, defending, or negotiating?

Every utterance carries some position, even when the speaker does not intend it.

For example:

“Obviously, this is wrong.”

This does more than disagree.

It positions the speaker as someone who sees clearly.
It positions the listener, perhaps, as someone who failed to see something obvious.
It raises distance.
It may reduce alignment.
It increases confidence, but possibly at the cost of relationship.

Compare:

“I think there may be a problem here.”

This positions the speaker more tentatively.
It leaves space for discussion.
It reduces threat.
It may preserve alignment, but it may also sound less decisive.

Positioning is therefore a central part of pragmatic language.

A sentence does not only mean.

It places.

7.3 Authority

Authority is one of the most visible forms of positioning.

Language can create, express, strengthen, soften, or challenge authority.

Compare:

“Do this by Friday.”

with:

“Could you do this by Friday?”

and:

“Would it make sense for you to take this by Friday?”

These sentences may point toward the same action, but they carry different authority positions.

The first is directive.
The second is a request.
The third is collaborative negotiation.

Authority may come from role, expertise, responsibility, ownership, institutional status, or situational necessity.

A manager may speak with role authority.

“Please send the final version by 5 p.m.”

An expert may speak with knowledge authority.

“This method is unlikely to work because the sample size is too small.”

A coordinator may speak with process authority.

“Let us pause here and decide who owns the next step.”

A witness may speak with evidence authority.

“I was present when the decision was made.”

Different kinds of authority require different language.

Role authority can direct.
Expert authority can advise or evaluate.
Process authority can organize.
Evidence authority can testify.

A speaker should know what kind of authority they are using.

7.4 Overclaiming Authority

Authority becomes fragile when language claims more authority than the speaker actually has.

For example:

“This is definitely the only correct solution.”

If the speaker lacks enough evidence, this overclaims certainty.

Another example:

“You must do it this way.”

If the speaker lacks role authority, this may sound controlling.

Overclaiming authority creates resistance because the listener senses a mismatch between language and position.

A better formulation may be:

“Based on what we know now, this seems to be the strongest option.”

or:

“I recommend this approach because it reduces the main risk.”

These sentences still carry judgment, but they do not pretend to absolute authority.

Good pragmatic language aligns authority with actual grounds.

The speaker should ask:

What gives me the right to say this?
Am I speaking from role, expertise, evidence, responsibility, or preference?
Does my language match that basis?

When authority and language are aligned, speech becomes more credible.

When they are misaligned, speech becomes unstable.

7.5 Softening Authority

Sometimes authority must be softened.

This does not mean abandoning authority.

It means expressing authority in a form that the relationship and situation can carry.

For example, a manager may say:

“I need you to revise this before it goes out.”

This is direct and appropriate if the task is operational.

But in a coaching situation, the manager may say:

“The main issue is structure. I would like you to revise the argument so the reader can follow the reasoning more easily.”

This still carries authority, but it explains the standard and supports learning.

Authority can be softened through:

  • reason

  • explanation

  • invitation

  • shared goal

  • acknowledgment

  • permission to respond

  • clear standards instead of personal force

Examples:

“Because this will go to the client, we need a higher level of precision.”

“I see the direction, but before approval, the evidence needs to be stronger.”

“Let us align on the standard before you revise.”

Softened authority is especially useful when the goal is not only compliance, but development, trust, or long-term cooperation.

7.6 Distance

Language also creates distance.

Distance refers to how close or far the speaker positions themselves from the listener emotionally, socially, or institutionally.

Compare:

“Fix this.”

with:

“Could you take another look at this?”

and:

“I think we may need to revisit this section together.”

The first creates distance and hierarchy.
The second is polite but still separate.
The third creates joint involvement.

Distance can be created by:

  • formal vocabulary

  • titles and roles

  • indirectness

  • absence of personal pronouns

  • passive voice

  • emotional neutrality

  • institutional phrasing

  • reduced self-disclosure

For example:

“Your request has been denied.”

This is distant and institutional.

“I cannot approve this request because it does not meet the criteria.”

This is still formal, but more personally accountable.

“I understand why you asked, but I cannot approve it under the current rules.”

This reduces distance by adding recognition and explanation.

Distance is not always bad.

High distance can be useful in:

  • legal communication

  • formal decisions

  • institutional procedures

  • emotionally charged conflict

  • professional boundaries

  • high-risk situations

Low distance can be useful in:

  • friendship

  • teaching

  • collaboration

  • repair

  • trust-building

  • creative work

The question is not whether distance is good or bad.

The question is whether the distance fits the speech situation.

7.7 Excessive Distance

Excessive distance can make speech feel cold, evasive, or bureaucratic.

For example:

“Mistakes were made.”

This sentence creates distance from responsibility.

It does not say:

  • who made the mistake

  • what happened

  • who was affected

  • what will be done

A more responsible version:

“We made an error in the final calculation, and I take responsibility for not catching it before delivery.”

This reduces distance and increases accountability.

Another example:

“Your performance has not met expectations.”

This may be appropriate in formal evaluation.

But if used without explanation, it may feel abstract and threatening.

A more useful version:

“The main issue is that the last three reports were submitted late, and two of them were missing the required evidence section.”

This reduces harmful distance by making the issue concrete.

Distance should protect professionalism, not hide responsibility.

7.8 Excessive Closeness

The opposite problem is excessive closeness.

A speaker may use overly familiar language in a situation that requires boundary, precision, or authority.

For example:

“Hey, maybe just try not to mess this up again.”

This is casual, but unclear and potentially disrespectful.

In a high-stakes work setting, better:

“For the next submission, please check the numbers against the source file before sending. Accuracy is required here because the report goes directly to the client.”

Excessive closeness may also blur roles.

For example, a leader who always speaks as a friend may struggle to set standards.

A teacher who avoids authority completely may fail to guide.

A doctor who speaks too casually about serious risk may reduce clarity.

Relational warmth is valuable.

But warmth should not erase responsibility.

7.9 Alignment

Alignment refers to whether language places speaker and listener on the same side, different sides, or opposing sides.

Compare:

“You failed to solve the problem.”

with:

“We have not solved the problem yet.”

The first positions the listener as the problem-bearer.
The second creates shared ownership.

Compare:

“Your idea does not work.”

with:

“The current version still has a risk we need to address.”

The first creates opposition between speaker and listener.
The second aligns both against the issue.

Alignment is one of the most powerful tools in pragmatic language.

It can turn conflict into cooperation.

A speaker can create alignment by using:

  • “we” language

  • shared goals

  • common standards

  • problem externalization

  • recognition of the listener’s intention

  • separation of person and issue

  • future-oriented framing

Examples:

“We both want this to be reliable.”

“The goal is not to reject the idea, but to make it stronger.”

“Let us treat this as a design problem, not a personal failure.”

These sentences position speaker and listener as collaborators.

7.10 Misleading Alignment

Alignment can also be manipulative or false.

For example:

“We all agree that this is the right direction.”

But perhaps not everyone agrees.

Or:

“We need to make sacrifices.”

But the sacrifice may fall on only one group.

Or:

“We are a family here.”

This may create emotional pressure while hiding unequal power or responsibility.

Alignment language is powerful because it creates belonging.

But it must be truthful.

False alignment damages trust.

A responsible speaker should ask:

Is this really shared?
Who is included in “we”?
Who carries the cost?
Who has the power to decide?
Who is being pressured by the language of unity?

Alignment should clarify shared purpose, not erase difference.

7.11 Opposition

Not all communication should maximize alignment.

Sometimes opposition must be made explicit.

For example:

“I disagree with this decision.”

“I cannot support this plan.”

“This violates the standard we agreed to.”

“I do not think this is safe.”

Opposition is necessary when values, evidence, safety, or responsibility are at stake.

But opposition can be designed in different ways.

Destructive opposition attacks the person:

“You clearly do not understand the issue.”

Constructive opposition addresses the claim, action, evidence, or standard:

“I disagree with the conclusion because the evidence does not support it.”

The second version positions the speaker as an evaluator of reasoning, not an attacker of identity.

Good opposition is clear about disagreement while disciplined about target.

It should specify:

  • what is being opposed

  • why it is being opposed

  • what standard is being used

  • what alternative is possible

  • whether the relationship remains intact

Example:

“I disagree with launching this week because the payment bug affects new users. I support the launch goal, but I do not think the current version meets the reliability standard.”

This sentence separates alignment and opposition.

The speaker aligns with the goal, but opposes the timing.

That distinction matters.

7.12 Confidence

Language also positions the speaker’s confidence.

A speaker may sound certain, tentative, doubtful, exploratory, cautious, or authoritative.

Compare:

“This will work.”

“This should work.”

“This might work.”

“This could work if the assumptions hold.”

“I am not sure this will work.”

Each sentence places the speaker differently in relation to certainty.

Confidence is not only emotional.

It is epistemic.

It shows how strongly the speaker claims to know.

Confidence can be expressed through:

  • modal verbs: must, should, might, could

  • adverbs: definitely, probably, possibly

  • verbs of cognition: know, think, believe, suspect, wonder

  • evidence phrases: based on the data, from what we have seen

  • hedges: seems, appears, may, likely

  • direct assertions: this is, this will, this means

A responsible speaker calibrates confidence according to evidence.

Too much confidence without evidence is dangerous.

Too little confidence when evidence is strong can weaken decision-making.

7.13 Overconfidence

Overconfidence appears when language is stronger than the evidence.

Examples:

“This will definitely succeed.”

“There is no risk.”

“Everyone will like this.”

“This is obviously the best option.”

Such language may create false certainty.

In high-stakes situations, overconfidence can produce poor decisions because it suppresses doubt, hides assumptions, and discourages challenge.

A more responsible version:

“This is our strongest option based on current evidence, but it depends on user adoption in the first month.”

This keeps confidence, but includes condition.

Another example:

“There is no risk.”

Better:

“We have not identified a major risk yet, but we still need to test the payment flow under real traffic.”

The second sentence is more accurate.

It distinguishes absence of known risk from proof of safety.

7.14 Underconfidence

Underconfidence appears when language is weaker than the speaker’s actual grounds.

Examples:

“I guess maybe this could be a problem.”

when the speaker has strong evidence that it is a problem.

Or:

“I am not sure, but perhaps we should consider…”

when urgent action is needed.

Underconfidence can be harmful because it hides important judgment.

It may cause others to ignore a real concern.

A more accurate version:

“I believe this is a serious problem. The evidence is that three users failed at the same step, and the error appears before payment confirmation.”

This sentence claims confidence and gives evidence.

Good language does not always soften itself.

Sometimes responsibility requires firmness.

7.15 Confidence Calibration

A useful confidence scale can guide speech:

Confidence Level Language Form Example
Unknown “We do not know yet.” “We do not know why the error occurs.”
Possible “It may be…” “It may be related to the login flow.”
Likely “It is likely…” “It is likely caused by the API change.”
Strongly Supported “The evidence suggests…” “The evidence suggests the issue affects new users only.”
Certain for Action “We should act as if…” “We should act as if this is a launch blocker.”
Decided “We will…” “We will delay launch until the payment issue is fixed.”

This scale helps prevent two errors:

  • pretending uncertainty is certainty

  • pretending evidence is weaker than it is

Confidence is not a personality style.

It is a responsibility to match language with grounds.

7.16 Relational Stance

Relational stance is the posture a speaker takes toward the listener.

A speaker may position themselves as:

  • helper

  • judge

  • peer

  • learner

  • leader

  • expert

  • challenger

  • protector

  • negotiator

  • outsider

  • subordinate

  • witness

The same content changes depending on stance.

Consider:

“This part is unclear.”

As a judge:

“This part is unclear and does not meet the standard.”

As a helper:

“This part may be hard for readers to follow. I can help you restructure it.”

As a peer:

“I had trouble following this part. Maybe we can simplify the sequence.”

As a learner:

“I am not sure I understand this part. Could you explain the connection?”

As an expert:

“The issue is that the claim is not supported by evidence.”

Each stance changes the relation.

The content overlaps, but the social position differs.

A good speaker chooses stance deliberately.

7.17 Stance Mismatch

Communication often fails because stance is mismatched.

A person may intend to help but sound like a judge.

“You should have done this differently.”

A person may intend to lead but sound uncertain.

“Maybe we can perhaps consider doing something.”

A person may intend to collaborate but sound controlling.

“Here is exactly how you should do it.”

A person may intend to be polite but sound evasive.

“It might not be completely ideal.”

when the real meaning is:

“This cannot be approved.”

Stance mismatch creates confusion because the listener receives a different social signal from the one intended.

A speaker should ask:

What stance am I taking?
Does my language actually express that stance?
Will the listener hear the stance I intend?

7.18 Positioning and Identity

Language also positions identity.

People use language to show what kind of person they are, or want to be seen as.

Someone may position themselves as rational:

“Let us look at the evidence.”

As decisive:

“We need to choose now.”

As careful:

“I do not want to move before we understand the risk.”

As humble:

“I may be missing something.”

As experienced:

“I have seen this pattern before.”

As aligned:

“I want this to work too.”

As independent:

“I cannot agree without stronger reasons.”

Identity positioning is unavoidable.

Every style of speech suggests something about the speaker.

But identity positioning becomes problematic when it overrides the actual task.

For example, a person may speak with excessive certainty in order to appear strong.

Or speak with excessive complexity in order to appear intelligent.

Or avoid disagreement in order to appear kind.

Or challenge everything in order to appear independent.

In such cases, language serves self-image more than communication.

Pragmatic maturity requires the speaker to ask:

Am I using language to serve the situation, or to protect an image of myself?

7.19 Positioning Others

Language does not only position the speaker.

It positions the listener.

A speaker can position the listener as:

  • capable or incapable

  • responsible or irresponsible

  • trusted or distrusted

  • included or excluded

  • junior or equal

  • guilty or mistaken

  • collaborator or obstacle

  • learner or expert

  • agent or victim

For example:

“You probably will not understand this.”

This positions the listener as incapable.

“You have handled similar issues before, so I would like your view.”

This positions the listener as capable and trusted.

Another example:

“You caused this problem.”

This positions the listener as guilty.

“Your decision contributed to this outcome, and we need to understand how.”

This still assigns responsibility, but with more analytical discipline.

How people are positioned affects how they respond.

A listener positioned as incompetent may become defensive.

A listener positioned as capable may rise to responsibility.

A listener positioned as enemy may resist.

A listener positioned as collaborator may engage.

Language shapes not only meaning, but social identity inside the interaction.

7.20 Positioning Through Pronouns

Pronouns are small words with large pragmatic force.

Compare:

“You need to fix this.”

“We need to fix this.”

“This needs to be fixed.”

These sentences position responsibility differently.

“You” assigns responsibility to the listener.

“We” creates shared responsibility.

“This needs to be fixed” removes explicit agent and focuses on the task.

Each may be appropriate in different situations.

If the listener truly owns the mistake:

“You need to correct the numbers before sending the report.”

If the issue is shared:

“We need to improve the review process.”

If the focus should stay on the object:

“This section needs revision.”

Pronouns should not be automatic.

They should match responsibility.

Misuse of “we” can hide accountability.

Misuse of “you” can create blame.

Misuse of passive structure can erase agency.

A responsible speaker chooses pronouns carefully.

7.21 Positioning Through Modality

Modality expresses necessity, possibility, permission, obligation, and recommendation.

Words such as must, should, can, could, may, might, need to, have to, and allowed to position both speaker and listener.

Compare:

“You must revise this.”

“You should revise this.”

“You could revise this.”

“You may revise this.”

“You might revise this.”

Each one changes the force.

“Must” creates obligation.
“Should” creates recommendation or norm.
“Could” creates possibility.
“May” grants permission or possibility.
“Might” weakens certainty.

Modality is central to positioning because it expresses how strongly reality, authority, or judgment presses on action.

For example:

“We must stop the release.”

This positions the issue as urgent and non-negotiable.

“We should stop the release.”

This positions the issue as strong recommendation.

“We might stop the release.”

This positions the issue as one possible option.

The facts may be similar, but the action pressure differs.

Pragmatic language requires modality discipline.

Use “must” only when obligation is real.
Use “should” when recommendation is strong but not absolute.
Use “could” when opening possibilities.
Use “might” when uncertainty remains.

7.22 Positioning Through Evaluation

Evaluation also positions people and objects.

Compare:

“This is bad.”

“This does not meet the standard.”

“This is not ready for client delivery.”

“This version needs stronger evidence before it can support the recommendation.”

The first is broad and judgmental.

The later versions are more specific and standard-based.

Evaluation can position the speaker as:

  • judge

  • expert

  • critic

  • coach

  • collaborator

  • authority

It can position the listener as:

  • failing

  • learning

  • improving

  • responsible

  • careless

  • capable

A useful principle:

Evaluate the object through standards, not the person through labels.

Instead of:

“You are careless.”

Say:

“The report contains three unchecked numbers, so the review process needs to be stricter before submission.”

This positions the issue as correctable.

It also preserves responsibility.

7.23 Positioning Through Certainty and Evidence

When speakers attach evidence to claims, they position themselves differently.

Compare:

“This is wrong.”

with:

“This is wrong because the conclusion does not follow from the data.”

and:

“I think this conclusion is not yet supported because the sample only includes returning users.”

The first relies on assertion.

The second relies on reasoning.

The third combines judgment with evidence and calibrated confidence.

Evidence-based speech positions the speaker as responsible and grounded.

It also gives the listener something to examine.

Pure assertion often creates status conflict.

Evidence moves the interaction toward shared evaluation.

This is especially important in disagreement.

A speaker who says:

“I disagree.”

may create opposition.

A speaker who says:

“I disagree because the evidence does not support the assumption that new users will complete onboarding.”

creates a path for discussion.

Evidence changes the social meaning of disagreement.

7.24 Positioning in Conflict

Conflict makes positioning especially visible.

In conflict, language can escalate or stabilize.

Escalating language positions the other side as enemy, irrational, careless, or morally defective.

Examples:

“You never listen.”

“This is completely your fault.”

“You clearly do not care.”

“Only an idiot would think this works.”

Stabilizing language names the issue without totalizing the person.

Examples:

“I do not think my concern was addressed.”

“This decision created a problem for the timeline.”

“I see the goal, but I disagree with the method.”

“Let us separate intention from impact.”

Conflict does not require softness.

It requires precision.

A speaker can be firm without being reckless.

For example:

“I cannot accept this version because it does not meet the agreed standard.”

This is firm.

It does not insult the listener.

Good conflict language positions the speaker as clear, responsible, and bounded.

It positions the listener as someone who can respond to the issue, not as someone being attacked as a whole person.

7.25 Positioning in Leadership

Leadership is largely a matter of language positioning.

A leader must often shift among several positions:

  • decision-maker

  • listener

  • coordinator

  • standard-setter

  • protector

  • learner

  • accountable owner

  • conflict manager

Different moments require different language.

When direction is needed:

“We will prioritize reliability over speed for this release.”

When listening is needed:

“Before we decide, I want to understand the strongest objection.”

When accountability is needed:

“I made the final call, and I take responsibility for that decision.”

When coordination is needed:

“You own the data check, Mei owns the client message, and I will review the final version.”

When uncertainty is real:

“We do not know the full cause yet, but we know enough to pause the release.”

Leadership fails when positioning is unstable.

A leader who never decides creates confusion.
A leader who only commands suppresses information.
A leader who avoids responsibility damages trust.
A leader who pretends certainty loses credibility.

Good leadership language positions authority, uncertainty, responsibility, and care in the right proportions.

7.26 Positioning in Learning

Learning situations also depend on positioning.

A learner can position themselves as passive:

“I do not understand.”

Or active:

“I understand the first step, but I lose the connection between the evidence and the conclusion.”

The second sentence positions the learner as someone diagnosing their own gap.

A teacher can position the learner as deficient:

“You are wrong again.”

Or developing:

“The concept is not stable yet. Let us rebuild it from the basic distinction.”

The second version preserves standards while supporting growth.

Learning requires a delicate position:

  • the learner must admit incompleteness

  • the teacher must hold standards

  • both must treat error as information

Good learning language separates identity from current capability.

Instead of:

“I am bad at this.”

Say:

“I do not yet have a stable method for doing this.”

This positions the problem as trainable.

Language changes the learner’s relationship to difficulty.

7.27 Positioning in Collaboration

Collaboration requires careful balance.

People must be able to contribute, disagree, ask, refuse, and commit without constantly threatening the relationship.

Useful collaborative positioning includes:

“Let me build on that.”

“I agree with the goal, but I see a risk in the method.”

“Can we separate the user problem from the implementation choice?”

“I may be missing something, but I think the dependency is unresolved.”

“What would make this option stronger?”

These sentences position the speaker as engaged rather than hostile.

They also position the listener as a contributor rather than an opponent.

Collaboration breaks down when language creates unnecessary hierarchy, vague agreement, hidden opposition, or personal defensiveness.

Good collaborative language keeps the shared object in the center.

The object may be:

  • the problem

  • the plan

  • the design

  • the decision

  • the evidence

  • the standard

  • the user need

  • the risk

When the shared object remains visible, people can disagree without losing the relationship.

7.28 Positioning and Face

In social interaction, people care about face.

Face refers to the public sense of dignity, competence, autonomy, and social value.

Language can protect face or threaten it.

Face-threatening speech includes:

“You do not know what you are doing.”

“This is embarrassing.”

“Everyone can see the problem except you.”

Face-preserving speech addresses the issue while reducing unnecessary humiliation:

“The current version has a structural problem that we can fix.”

“There is a gap in the evidence section.”

“Let us review the decision process and see where the assumption entered.”

Face preservation is not dishonesty.

It is the discipline of correcting without unnecessary social damage.

However, face-saving should not hide reality.

A sentence can be both clear and respectful:

“This cannot be approved in its current form because two required sections are missing.”

This protects the standard while avoiding personal attack.

7.29 Positioning and Power

Power changes how language is heard.

The same sentence from a powerful speaker may carry more force than intended.

For example, when a manager says:

“It would be nice to have this by tomorrow.”

An employee may hear:

“This is required by tomorrow.”

The manager may think they made a casual suggestion.

But because of power difference, the sentence functions as direction.

Power increases pragmatic force.

This means higher-power speakers have greater responsibility for clarity.

They should not rely too heavily on hints when others may experience hints as commands.

A leader should distinguish:

“This is a suggestion.”

from:

“This is a decision.”

and:

“This is a requirement.”

Lower-power speakers face a different challenge.

They may need to disagree, refuse, or ask for clarification while managing risk.

For example:

“I understand the direction. I have one concern about the timeline: if we keep the current scope, I do not think Friday delivery is realistic.”

This sentence is respectful but clear.

It positions the speaker as responsible, not rebellious.

7.30 Repositioning

Repositioning is the act of changing the relational structure of an interaction.

For example, a conversation may begin as blame:

“You caused the delay.”

A speaker may reposition it as process analysis:

“Let us identify where the delay entered the workflow and what needs to change.”

A conversation may begin as personal failure:

“I am terrible at speaking English.”

It can be repositioned as skill development:

“The current gap is real-time organization of meaning, not personal inability.”

A conversation may begin as opposition:

“Your plan is wrong.”

It can be repositioned as shared evaluation:

“We both want the plan to work. The question is whether this assumption is safe.”

Repositioning is not avoiding the issue.

It changes the social geometry so the issue can be handled more intelligently.

A mature speaker can notice when the current position is unhelpful and shift it.

7.31 Practical Positioning Questions

Before important speech, ask:

Authority:
What right or basis do I have to say this?

Distance:
How close or formal should this speech be?

Alignment:
Am I positioning us on the same side, different sides, or opposite sides?

Confidence:
How certain am I, and does my language match the evidence?

Stance:
Am I speaking as a judge, helper, peer, expert, leader, learner, or negotiator?

Responsibility:
Who is being positioned as responsible?

Face:
Does this language correct the issue without unnecessary humiliation?

Power:
Will my role make this speech stronger than I intend?

These questions turn positioning from instinct into design.

7.32 Practical Templates

7.32.1 Authority With Reason

I am recommending [action]
because [evidence/reason],
and the standard we need to meet is [standard].

Example:

“I am recommending that we delay the release because the payment bug affects new users, and the standard we need to meet is a reliable first purchase flow.”

7.32.2 Alignment Before Disagreement

I agree with [shared goal],
but I disagree with [specific method/claim]
because [reason].

Example:

“I agree with the goal of moving faster, but I disagree with cutting the review step because this release has payment risk.”

7.32.3 Confidence Calibration

Based on [evidence],
I think [claim] is [possible/likely/strongly supported],
but [uncertainty/condition] remains.

Example:

“Based on the last three tests, I think the issue is likely in the API layer, but we still need to verify the input format.”

7.32.4 Respectful Correction

The issue is not [person/identity],
but [object/process/standard].
The specific problem is [problem],
and the next step is [action].

Example:

“The issue is not your effort, but the structure of the report. The specific problem is that the evidence does not support the recommendation, and the next step is to rebuild the argument around three claims.”

7.32.5 Repositioning Conflict

Let us move this from [unhelpful frame]
to [more useful frame].
The question is not [old question],
but [better question].

Example:

“Let us move this from blame to process analysis. The question is not who looks bad, but where the workflow allowed the error to pass.”

7.33 Failure Modes

7.33.1 False Authority

The speaker uses command language without sufficient authority.

“You must do this my way.”

Result:

Resistance, resentment, or status conflict.

7.33.2 Hidden Authority

The speaker has authority but hides the directive as a suggestion.

“Maybe someone could finish this tonight.”

Result:

Confusion about responsibility.

7.33.3 Excessive Distance

The speaker uses abstract language to avoid responsibility.

“Errors occurred.”

Result:

Lack of accountability.

7.33.4 False Alignment

The speaker uses “we” to hide unequal burden.

“We need to work harder.”

Result:

Resentment if only some people carry the cost.

7.33.5 Overconfidence

The speaker states uncertain claims as facts.

“This will definitely work.”

Result:

Poor risk management.

7.33.6 Underconfidence

The speaker weakens a necessary warning.

“Maybe this could be an issue.”

Result:

Serious concerns may be ignored.

7.33.7 Personalizing the Issue

The speaker turns a correctable problem into an identity judgment.

“You are careless.”

Result:

Defensiveness, shame, or conflict.

7.33.8 Erasing Agency

The speaker uses passive or vague language when responsibility should be named.

“The decision was made.”

Result:

No one knows who owns the consequence.

7.34 Minimal Operational Distinction

A simple model can guide this chapter:

Authority: Who has the right to speak or decide?
Distance: How close or formal is the relation?
Alignment: Are we together, separate, or opposed?
Confidence: How strongly is the claim held?
Stance: What role is the speaker taking?
Responsibility: Who is positioned as accountable?

Positioning through language means shaping these dimensions through words.

The speaker may not intend to position anyone.

But language will do it anyway.

Therefore, pragmatic competence requires deliberate positioning.

7.35 Chapter Summary

Language does not only express content.

It positions people.

Through word choice, pronouns, modality, tone, evidence, directness, and evaluation, speakers establish authority, distance, alignment, confidence, and relational stance.

These positions affect how speech is received.

A statement may sound like command, suggestion, support, criticism, judgment, invitation, or attack depending on how it positions speaker and listener.

Good pragmatic language aligns position with reality.

Authority should match actual authority.
Distance should fit the relationship.
Alignment should be truthful.
Confidence should match evidence.
Stance should match purpose.
Responsibility should be clearly located.

The central discipline is this:

Do not ask only what your sentence says.
Ask where it places you and the listener.

Because in real interaction, language does not float above the relationship.

It rearranges the relationship.
