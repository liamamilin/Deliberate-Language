7  Evaluation Under Criteria

7.1 From Preference to Judgment

Evaluation begins when alternatives are judged under standards. The previous chapters established that alternatives must first be compared and trade-offs named. This chapter completes the movement of Part II by asking how alternatives become evaluable under criteria.

Evaluation is not the same as liking. Preference may be real, but preference alone is not yet judgment. Judgment requires a standard: by what criterion is this option better, under which goal is this option preferable, what evidence supports the evaluation, and what would make it wrong? Preference says “I like this option.” Evaluation says “This option performs better under the criteria that matter here.” Judgment says “Given the goal, criteria, constraints, and trade-offs, this is the option that should be chosen.”

7.2 When Evaluation Frameworks May Mislead

The evaluation matrix and criteria-weighting methods in this chapter are powerful tools, but they carry risks that must be recognized.

The first risk is quantification bias. Assigning numbers to criteria and weights can create an illusion of precision where none exists. A score of 7.3 versus 7.1 looks decisive, but the difference may be noise. The matrix is a tool for making reasoning visible, not a calculator that produces the correct answer. The thinker must remain aware of where the numbers come from and how sensitive the conclusion is to small changes in weights or scores.

The second risk is the incommensurability of values. Some values cannot be meaningfully compared on a single scale. How does one weigh dignity against efficiency, or justice against speed? Attempting to score such values may distort them rather than clarify the decision. In such cases, the evaluation framework may need to acknowledge that the choice involves a genuine value conflict that cannot be resolved by weighting but must be decided by judgment.

The third risk is group distortion. When evaluation frameworks are used in groups, social dynamics can override the structure. Participants may adjust their scores to align with powerful members, avoid giving low scores to popular options, or treat the matrix as a ritual that justifies a decision already made. The framework does not protect against these dynamics unless the group is explicit about them and has processes to counteract bias.

The fourth risk is premature closure. A completed evaluation matrix can make the decision feel final, causing the group to stop considering new information or alternative framings. The framework should be treated as a living structure that can be revised as understanding deepens, not as a verdict that closes the discussion.

7.3 Criteria, Weighting, and Evaluation Matrices

A criterion is a standard used to evaluate an option. Common criteria include cost, speed, safety, quality, reversibility, strategic fit, learning value, and risk exposure. A criterion must serve the goal: a criterion irrelevant to the goal may produce a precise but meaningless evaluation. Explicit standards are named and can be examined; hidden standards operate beneath the surface and cannot be challenged until articulated.

Criteria can be weighted to reflect their relative importance; not all criteria carry equal weight in every decision. A threshold criterion sets a minimum acceptable level below which an option is excluded; a ranking criterion compares options along a continuum. An evaluation matrix combines options, criteria, weights, and scores into a single structure that forces explicit judgment: criteria must be named, weights assigned, and each option scored. The matrix is a tool for making reasoning visible, not a replacement for judgment.

Good evaluation language names criteria, connects them to the goal, shows performance, and states the conclusion with supporting reasoning. Bad evaluation language uses vague standards, applies criteria inconsistently, treats preference as evidence, or uses false precision. An evaluation matrix that reveals where reasoning is strong and where it is uncertain supports more honest judgment than a simple assertion of preference.

7.4 Chapter Summary

This chapter has studied evaluation as the process by which preference becomes judgment under explicit criteria. Evaluation requires standards that are connected to the goal and applied consistently across alternatives. Criteria can be weighted and distinguished as thresholds or ranking dimensions. An evaluation matrix makes the structure of judgment visible. Good evaluation language shows the reasoning path; bad evaluation language hides it.

7.4.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied whenever you evaluate alternatives.

First, never confuse preference with judgment. “I like this” is not an evaluation. An evaluation names the criteria and shows how the option performs against them. Second, every criterion must serve the goal. A precise criterion that is irrelevant to what you are trying to achieve produces a meaningless evaluation. Always ask: does this criterion connect to the goal? Third, make your standards explicit. Hidden standards cannot be examined, challenged, or balanced against other criteria. If you reject an option because it “feels wrong,” translate that feeling into a criterion so it can be discussed. Fourth, use weights to reflect priority. Not all criteria matter equally; assign weight according to the goal. Fifth, an evaluation matrix is a tool for making reasoning visible, not a machine that produces answers. The scores depend on your interpretations; the weights reflect your priorities. Be honest about where the numbers come from and where uncertainty remains. Sixth, when criteria conflict, return to the goal. The goal is the anchor; it determines which criterion should govern when they pull in different directions. With evaluation complete, the next challenge is uncertainty and partial knowledge, studied in Part III (Chapter 7).

7.4.2 🎯 行动指南

今日练习：为一个重要决策建立评价矩阵

选一个你当前面临的多选项决策（至少3个选项）。建立评价矩阵：

  1. 列出准则：4-6条，连接到一个具体目标
  2. 区分阈值型与排名型：如果某选项不满足阈值，直接排除（不用打分）
  3. 分配权重：最重要的准则权重最高，总和=100%
  4. 打分：1-5分（或1-10分），对每个选项在每个准则上打分
  5. 计算加权总分

完成后，做”敏感性测试”：把最重要的权重加减10%，看看排名会不会变。如果会变，说明你的判断对权重很敏感——你需要的不是更精确的数字，而是更清晰的价值排序。

行为陷阱预警： - 不要在有了偏好的选项后才构建矩阵——那样准则会偏向它。先列准则再看选项。 - 分数不是答案，只是你推理的可视化。如果矩阵的结果和你的直觉冲突，不要自动相信矩阵——去检查直觉在告诉你的东西是否矩阵漏掉了某个重要维度。 - “7.3 vs 7.1” 这样的微小差异通常没有意义。关注的是哪个选项在最重要的2-3个准则上领先。
