Part II — Speech Action and Intention

Orientation

Language does not merely describe reality.

It acts within reality.

When people speak, they are not only transmitting information. They are doing things: asking, refusing, promising, warning, agreeing, disagreeing, inviting, authorizing, apologizing, accusing, comforting, positioning, and committing themselves to future action.

A sentence may appear to carry content, but in actual use, it often performs a function.

To say:

“I will send it tomorrow.”

is not only to describe a future event.

It is to make a commitment.

To say:

“Could you close the door?”

is not only to ask about ability.

It is to make a request.

To say:

“I am sorry.”

is not only to report an emotional state.

It is to perform an act of repair.

This part examines language as action.

From Meaning to Function

Part I examined the situation of speech and the way meaning exceeds the sentence.

Part II moves one step further.

It asks:

What is the speaker doing by saying this?

This question is central to pragmatic language.

A speech act cannot be understood only by analyzing its grammar. It must be understood by identifying its function within a situation.

The same sentence may perform different actions depending on intention, context, relationship, and stakes.

For example:

“You are here early.”

This may function as:

  • a simple observation

  • a compliment

  • a question

  • a suspicion

  • a criticism

  • an invitation to explain

The literal content remains stable.

The speech action changes.

Language as Action

Speech can perform many kinds of action.

It can:

  • give information

  • ask for information

  • request action

  • issue commands

  • refuse requests

  • make promises

  • express agreement

  • express disagreement

  • give permission

  • withdraw permission

  • assign responsibility

  • create obligation

  • repair damage

  • define social position

These actions are not secondary to language.

They are part of what language is for.

In human life, many important realities are created or changed through speech:

“I agree.”
“You are hired.”
“I quit.”
“I promise.”
“You are responsible for this.”
“The meeting is canceled.”
“We choose option B.”

Such sentences do not merely describe decisions.

They enact them.

Intention and Recognition

Speech action depends on intention, but intention alone is not enough.

For a speech act to work, the listener must usually recognize what the speaker is trying to do.

A request succeeds only if it is understood as a request.
A warning succeeds only if it is understood as a warning.
A promise succeeds only if it is understood as a promise.
An apology succeeds only if it is understood as an apology.

This means that speech action exists between speaker and listener.

It is not located only inside the speaker’s mind.

A speaker may intend to comfort, but the listener may hear condescension.
A speaker may intend to suggest, but the listener may hear command.
A speaker may intend to refuse gently, but the listener may hear uncertainty.
A speaker may intend to criticize the work, but the listener may hear an attack on the person.

The success of speech action depends on alignment between:

Intention → Expression → Recognition → Response

When this chain breaks, communication fails.

The Function of This Part

This part studies how utterances perform actions.

It focuses on several core speech functions:

  • informing

  • requesting

  • refusing

  • promising

  • positioning

These are not merely isolated speech types.

They are foundational operations of human interaction.

To inform is to change what another person knows.
To request is to invite or pressure another person to act.
To refuse is to set a boundary.
To promise is to create future obligation.
To position is to define oneself or others within a social field.

Each function has its own structure, risks, and responsibility.

A person who cannot request clearly will struggle to coordinate.
A person who cannot refuse clearly will lose boundaries.
A person who promises carelessly will damage trust.
A person who positions poorly will mismanage identity, authority, and relationship.

Speech action is therefore not only linguistic.

It is practical, social, and ethical.

Speech Acts and Responsibility

Once language is understood as action, speech becomes a matter of responsibility.

Words can create expectations.
Words can assign roles.
Words can invite trust.
Words can damage trust.
Words can open possibilities.
Words can close them.

A speaker is responsible not only for whether a sentence is grammatically correct, but for what the sentence does in context.

This is especially important when speech creates obligation or consequence.

For example:

“I will try.”

This may seem harmless.

But depending on the situation, it may be heard as:

  • a weak promise

  • a non-commitment

  • a polite refusal

  • an expression of uncertainty

  • a way to delay decision

If the stakes are high, such ambiguity can become costly.

Deliberate speech requires the speaker to know what action their words are performing.

Structural Logic of Part II

The movement of this part is:

Utterance
→ Function
→ Intention
→ Recognition
→ Obligation
→ Social Position

First, we examine how speech carries function beyond content.

Then we study specific speech actions: informing, requesting, refusing, and promising.

Finally, we examine positioning: how language places people within relationships, roles, authority structures, and social expectations.

The central claim is simple:

To speak is not only to say something.
To speak is to do something.

Transition

Once language is understood as action, the next question becomes more precise:

What kinds of action can speech perform?

The following chapters examine this question through the basic operations of pragmatic life: informing, requesting, refusing, promising, and positioning.
