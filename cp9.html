10  Assumptions, Constraints, and Hidden Dependencies

10.1 The Hidden Architecture of Judgment

Every judgment rests on conditions. Some conditions are visible, some are hidden, some are chosen, some are imposed, some are stable, and some may collapse under pressure. A decision may look reasonable on the surface, but its real strength depends on the assumptions, constraints, and dependencies underneath it.

The previous two chapters examined uncertainty and risk. Chapter 7 studied how language represents what is known, unclear, assumed, and unknown. Chapter 8 studied how language handles possible outcomes, likelihood, consequence, and scenario projection. This chapter moves deeper into the hidden architecture of judgment: the assumptions we rely on, the constraints we operate within, and the dependencies that silently support or weaken our options.

A weak decision asks which option looks best. A stronger decision asks what must be true for this option to work. This second question reveals the hidden structure beneath an option: a choice is not only a visible path but also a set of conditions that must hold. Deliberate language brings these conditions into view, making judgment inspectable and providing a foundation for testing and revision.

10.2 Assumptions as Hidden Foundations

An assumption is something treated as true for the purpose of reasoning, even though it has not been fully proven. Assumptions are unavoidable; no decision can wait until every fact is certain. Every plan requires some beliefs about the future, other people, available resources, timing, capability, demand, and consequence. The problem is not that we assume; the problem is that we often do not know what we are assuming.

A hidden assumption may control the entire decision without appearing in the language of the decision. The statement “This project will succeed” may contain assumptions about user need, team capability, budget adequacy, timeline realism, market stability, motivation continuity, and the correctness of the problem definition. If these assumptions are strong, the judgment may be strong. If they are weak, the conclusion may be weak even if the sentence sounds confident.

Deliberate language distinguishes facts from assumptions. A fact says “This has happened.” An assumption says “We are treating this as likely or true for now.” A forecast says “We expect this to happen.” A hope says “We want this to happen.” These categories are often confused: people treat assumptions as facts, forecasts as guarantees, and hopes as evidence. Deliberate language keeps them separate by marking the status of each claim.

Assumptions vary in strength. A strong assumption is supported by evidence, experience, or structural logic; a weak assumption rests on optimism, convention, or convenience. The most critical type is the load-bearing assumption: one that, if false, changes the entire judgment. Identifying load-bearing assumptions is a central task of deliberate reasoning. The question “What must be true for this decision to be correct?” exposes the assumptions that matter most.

10.3 Constraints and Dependencies

Constraints are the limits around choice. They define what is not possible, what cannot be changed, and what must be accepted as given. Hard constraints are fixed: budget limits, legal requirements, physical realities, and deadlines that cannot be moved. Soft constraints can be negotiated, redesigned, or relaxed: preferred timelines, conventional approaches, or comfort zones.

A constraint is not the same as an assumption. A constraint defines the boundary of the possible; an assumption defines what is treated as true within that boundary. Both structure the decision space, but in different ways. A constraint that is treated as hard when it is actually soft may unnecessarily narrow the option set. A constraint that is genuinely hard must be accepted as a boundary condition.

A dependency is something an option relies on. A hidden dependency is a reliance that has not been named. The plan may depend on one person’s availability, on the platform not changing its rules, on visa approval, on data quality, on infrastructure stability, or on another party’s willingness to cooperate. Dependencies matter because they reveal where control is limited. A plan may look strong internally but fragile when dependencies are exposed.

Dependencies can be internal or external. Internal dependencies are within the decision-maker’s control: personal discipline, preparation, and capability. External dependencies are outside direct control: other people, institutions, market conditions, and natural events. The most fragile plans are those that assume external dependencies will hold without verification, fallback, or monitoring.

A dependency chain occurs when one dependency rests on another. If A depends on B, and B depends on C, then the failure of C cascades through the entire chain. The weakest link in the dependency chain determines the overall reliability of the plan. Identifying the chain and its weakest link allows the decision-maker to strengthen, replace, or monitor the critical dependency.

10.4 Testing and Reducing Dependencies

Assumptions can be tested. A testable assumption is one that can be examined before full commitment: running a small experiment, gathering data, consulting an expert, or creating a prototype. Untestable assumptions must be monitored: tracked over time with signals that indicate whether they remain valid.

Constraints can sometimes be relaxed. A constraint that appears fixed may be negotiable under the right conditions. The question “Is this constraint real or assumed?” is one of the most powerful in deliberation. Many decisions are constrained by assumptions disguised as realities.

Dependencies can be reduced. A dependency on a single person can be reduced by distributing knowledge; a dependency on a single supplier can be reduced by identifying alternatives; a dependency on a specific condition can be reduced by designing the plan to work under a wider range of conditions. Reducing dependencies increases the robustness of a plan without necessarily increasing its cost.

Bad language around assumptions, constraints, and dependencies treats them as identical or ignores them entirely. A statement that says “This plan will work” without naming any assumption is incomplete. A decision that treats all constraints as fixed is unnecessarily narrow. A plan that ignores dependencies is built on sand. Good language distinguishes these categories, names the load-bearing elements, and prepares responses for their failure.

10.5 Chapter Summary

This chapter has studied the hidden architecture beneath judgment: assumptions, constraints, and dependencies. Assumptions are what we treat as true without full proof; they vary in strength, and the load-bearing assumption is the one that, if false, changes everything. Language must distinguish facts from assumptions, forecasts from hopes.

Constraints are the limits around choice. Hard constraints are fixed; soft constraints can be negotiated or relaxed. The failure to distinguish them can unnecessarily narrow the decision space. Dependencies are what options rely on; hidden dependencies are reliances that have not been named. Dependency chains reveal where control is limited and where fragility lies.

Assumptions can be tested, constraints can sometimes be relaxed, and dependencies can be reduced. Good language names assumptions explicitly, distinguishes constraint types, and maps dependency structures.

10.5.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied whenever you examine the foundations of a judgment.

First, every judgment rests on assumptions. Your first question about any plan should not be “does this look good?” but “what must be true for this to work?” Second, identify the load-bearing assumption: the one that, if false, changes everything. Test this assumption before committing to the plan. Third, distinguish facts from assumptions, forecasts from hopes. A fact says “this has happened.” An assumption says “we are treating this as true for now.” A forecast says “we expect this to happen.” A hope says “we want this to happen.” Confusing these categories produces fragile reasoning. Fourth, distinguish hard constraints from soft constraints. A hard constraint is genuinely fixed; a soft constraint may be negotiable. Treating a soft constraint as hard unnecessarily narrows your options. Treating a hard constraint as soft leads to wasted effort. Fifth, map your dependencies. Every option relies on something: people, systems, conditions, timing. Hidden dependencies are the most dangerous because you do not prepare for their failure. Name them, rank them by fragility, and create fallbacks for the most critical ones. With uncertainty, risk, assumptions, and dependencies made visible, the next part (Chapter 10) moves from information to judgment: how evidence becomes a grounded conclusion.

10.5.2 🎯 行动指南

今日练习：挖掘一个计划中的承重假设

选一个你正在依赖的计划（可以是工作项目、学习计划、关系决定）。写下：

这个计划成功，取决于以下条件成立： 1. ____（如果错了，影响：致命/严重/轻微） 2. ____（如果错了，影响：致命/严重/轻微） 3. ____（如果错了，影响：致命/严重/轻微）

标注其中哪一个是承重假设——那个如果错了，整个计划就垮掉的前提。现在问：有什么办法在投入大资源之前测试这个假设？

然后做依赖关系图： > 我能控制的部分：____ > 我不能控制但可以影响的部分：____ > 我完全不能控制的部分：____

对于完全不能控制的依赖——你至少可以设置一个预警信号（“如果X发生，说明这个依赖出了问题”）。

行为调整提醒： - 当你听到”这计划很好的时候”，自动追问：“必须什么条件成立，这个计划才能成功？” - 每次被拒绝或计划失败时，不要只归咎于执行——也检查是不是一个假设错了。 - 区分”事实”和”假设”的日常练习：在一天中，注意自己说”我知道…“的时刻——那是一句经得起检验的事实，还是一个此刻被当作事实的假设？
