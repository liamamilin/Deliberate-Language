2  Framing the Problem

2.1 Problem Framing as the First Act of Deliberation

Deliberate thinking does not begin with solving. It begins with framing. Before a person compares alternatives, evaluates risks, forms judgment, or chooses a strategy, something more basic has already happened: a situation has been turned into a problem. This transformation is not automatic. Reality presents events, pressures, symptoms, conflicts, failures, desires, and disturbances, but none of these becomes a problem until language gives it form.

A problem is not merely something unpleasant, difficult, or demanding of attention. It is a structured object of thought. To frame a problem is to decide what the issue is, where its boundary lies, what caused or shaped it, who or what is involved, what matters about it, and what kind of response it calls for. This means problem framing is not a neutral introduction to thinking. It is already an act of judgment. The way a problem is stated determines what can be seen, what can be ignored, what can be changed, and what kinds of solutions appear reasonable.

The purpose of this chapter is to show how language constructs problems, how a well-formed problem frame differs from a vague or misleading one, and what methods can be used to turn inchoate concern into a workable object of deliberation. A poorly framed problem can make excellent reasoning useless. A well-framed problem does not solve the issue by itself, but it gives thought a stable object to work on.

2.2 Problems Are Linguistic Constructions

In ordinary speech, people often treat problems as if they simply exist. They say: the problem is money, or time, or motivation, or communication, or the market. These statements feel clear because they name something, but naming is not yet framing. To name a problem is to point toward an area of concern; to frame a problem is to define its structure.

Consider the phrase: “The problem is time.” This sentence is too compressed. It may refer to many different structures: there is not enough time, time is being used inefficiently, priorities are unclear, tasks are overcommitted, deadlines are unrealistic, sequencing is wrong, attention is fragmented, or the wrong work is being done at the wrong stage. Each formulation opens a different path of thought. If the problem is insufficient time, the solution may be to reduce scope. If the problem is poor sequencing, the solution may be to reorder work. If the problem is unclear priorities, the solution may be to define criteria. The same situation can generate different problems depending on how it is framed.

This is the first principle of deliberate language: a problem is not only found in reality; it is constructed through language. A problem frame selects, organizes, and emphasizes. It defines the object of concern, draws boundaries around the issue, identifies relevant causes, assigns agency or responsibility, suggests what kind of action is appropriate, and implies what would count as improvement. Even a short sentence may imply an entire architecture of thought. The statement “The team is moving too slowly” contains hidden assumptions about desirable speed, the unit of analysis, the relevant dimension of evaluation, and the direction of improvement. A frame makes these assumptions visible or leaves them hidden depending on how carefully it is constructed.

A problem frame is useful when it helps thought contact the real structure of the situation. A problem frame is harmful when it compresses complexity into a label, confuses symptoms with causes, or smuggles a preferred solution into the statement before the issue has been examined.

2.3 The Structure of a Problem Frame

A complete problem frame contains several elements, each answering a specific question about the situation. The following table summarizes these elements and their functions.

Element Question Function
Object What is the issue about? Defines the center of attention
Boundary Where does the problem begin and end? Prevents uncontrolled expansion
Condition What is the current state? Describes the situation
Standard Compared to what is this a problem? Establishes why it matters
Cause What produces or sustains it? Guides explanation
Agent Who or what can act? Defines responsibility and leverage
Consequence What happens if it continues? Establishes stakes
Direction What kind of change is needed? Opens the path toward solution

A strong problem frame makes these elements explicit. A weak problem frame leaves them hidden, causing reasoning to proceed on unstable ground. For example, a vague statement like “Students are lazy” compresses object, cause, and agent into a label without specifying the condition, standard, or direction. A stronger frame would identify the specific condition, the standard being violated, and the kind of change required.

The standard behind a problem is especially critical because something becomes a problem only in relation to an expectation. The statement “The project took three months” is not itself a problem. It becomes one only when compared against an expected duration. A vague standard produces vague judgment. Deliberate language requires the speaker to ask: what is the expected state, who defines that expectation, is the standard realistic and measurable, and is it appropriate for the context? Without a clear standard, the problem cannot be evaluated.

A practical grammar for constructing a problem frame can be expressed as: In [context], [subject] experiences [undesired condition] because [cause, constraint, or uncertainty], which affects [stakes], so the decision is [what must be clarified, changed, or chosen]. This structure does not need to be used mechanically every time, but it reveals what a complete problem frame typically contains.

2.4 Symptoms, Causes, and Boundaries

One of the most common failures in deliberate thinking is confusing symptoms with problems. A symptom is what appears; a problem is the structured condition that requires attention; a cause is what produces or maintains that condition. When a symptom is mistaken for the problem, action becomes shallow. An organization may respond to declining sales with more marketing, but if the real issue is product-market mismatch, more marketing only accelerates exposure to a weak offer.

A problem without boundaries becomes unmanageable. If the boundary is too narrow, the real issue is missed. If the boundary is too wide, the problem becomes impossible to act on. Consider the statement “I cannot learn English well.” This frame is too broad; it may include pronunciation, vocabulary, grammar, listening, reading, speaking, writing, confidence, learning method, or daily environment. A better frame would narrow the domain: “My spoken English is weak because I cannot quickly organize simple explanations in real time.” This frame defines the domain, the specific function, the difficulty, and the likely direction, making the problem actionable.

Not every discomfort is a problem in the deliberate sense. Discomfort is felt; a problem is formulated. Discomfort may be the starting signal, but it is not yet an object of judgment. The statement “I feel stuck” is emotionally real but structurally incomplete. It may become different problem frames: unclear goals, too many paths without criteria, lack of a concrete next action, avoidance of an uncertain decision, or an environment that does not support the intended direction. The conversion of discomfort into a workable structure is central to deliberate language: language takes a vague pressure and turns it into a defined object of thought.

2.5 Types of Problem Frames and Reframing

Different problems require different forms of framing. A problem may be framed as a gap, a conflict, a constraint, a risk, a failure, an opportunity, an ambiguity, or a dependency. Each form creates a different reasoning path. For example, “We do not have enough funding” is a constraint frame, while “We need to find a path that produces learning before capital runs out” is a strategic frame. The second formulation does not deny the constraint but organizes thought around action and sequencing.

Every problem frame implies an action path. A frame aimed at personality produces moral judgment; a frame aimed at process produces operational redesign; a frame aimed at incentives produces structural intervention; a frame aimed at goals produces clarification. When people disagree about solutions, they often disagree earlier than they realize: they are operating inside different problem frames.

Reframing is the act of changing the structure of the problem. It does not mean pretending the issue is positive or using softer language. It means altering the object, boundary, cause, standard, or action direction so that thought can contact the situation more accurately. A useful reframe may move from symptom to cause, from blame to structure, from event to pattern, from individual to system, from impossibility to constraint, or from vague concern to concrete gap. For example, “I am bad at writing” is a self-labeling frame. A reframe might be: “I lack a stable process for turning scattered ideas into structured paragraphs.” This makes the problem operational.

Bad frames that commonly cause damage include the label frame (compressing complex situations into fixed labels), the symptom frame (treating visible signals as the problem itself), the blame frame (organizing issues around fault rather than structure), the false choice frame (presenting binary options where a wider space exists), the totalizing frame (expanding the problem until it cannot be handled), and the solution-first frame (smuggling a preferred answer into the problem statement). Good framing delays solution long enough to understand the problem.

2.6 A Practical Method for Framing Problems

A useful problem-framing process moves through seven questions. First, what is happening? Begin with observation rather than interpretation, focusing on signals, changes, repetitions, and abnormalities. Second, why is this considered a problem? Identify the standard being violated, the goal being blocked, or the value being threatened. Third, what is the object of the problem? Determine whether the issue is about a person, a process, a system, a relationship, a resource, a constraint, or a mismatch. Changing the object changes the problem. Fourth, where is the boundary? Define scope by asking what is included, what is excluded, and what level is being worked at. Fifth, what might be causing or sustaining it? Move from symptom to structure by examining conditions, incentives, dependencies, assumptions, and feedback loops. Sixth, what action paths does this frame imply? If the frame is accepted, what will be attempted, who will be asked to change, and what risks does the frame create? Seventh, what alternative frames are possible? Generate competing formulations to test whether the first frame is the best frame.

Consider how a vague statement can be developed through multiple frames. The statement “I cannot make progress” may become a motivation problem, directing action toward discipline and routines; a goal problem, directing action toward clarification of desired outcomes and success criteria; or a decision space problem, directing action toward listing alternatives and defining criteria for choosing among them. Each frame may be partially true. The task is not to choose one instantly but to test which frame best explains the situation and opens useful action.

2.7 Responsibility, Time Horizon, and Level of Analysis

Problem framing also distributes responsibility. A frame can place responsibility on the individual, the team, the system, leadership, the environment, or external forces. The statement “The worker made a mistake” assigns responsibility to the individual, while “The process allowed a single mistake to cause system failure” assigns responsibility to process design. The point is not to remove individual responsibility but to locate it accurately. A mature frame can hold multiple layers: the individual made an error, but the system lacked safeguards and the organization had normalized a risky workflow.

Problems also change depending on time horizon. A short-term frame asks what must be fixed now; a medium-term frame asks what pattern must be changed; a long-term frame asks what structure must be redesigned. A missed deadline may be framed short-term as recovery, medium-term as estimation weakness, or long-term as planning system redesign. Bad deliberation often occurs when these horizons are confused: using long-term philosophical analysis when immediate action is required, or applying short-term fixes when structural redesign is needed.

A problem may also exist at different levels of analysis. In a software product, a failure may be framed at the level of interface, feature, user workflow, product positioning, technical architecture, team process, business model, or market timing. The art of framing is not always to go deeper; sometimes the immediate level is sufficient. But deliberate thinking must know which level it is operating on. The central question is: at what level must the problem be framed for action to be effective? If the frame is too shallow, action treats symptoms; if the frame is too deep, action becomes abstract and slow.

Problem framing is the gate through which the rest of deliberation passes. Once a problem is framed, goals can be clarified, criteria can be defined, options can be generated, alternatives can be compared, risks can be assessed, judgments can be made, and plans can be formed. But if the frame is unstable, everything downstream becomes unstable. A deliberate problem frame can be built using a template: The issue is not simply [surface symptom]. The deeper problem is [structured problem], in the context of [situation], where [subject/system] is trying to [goal] but faces [constraint/conflict/uncertainty], leading to [consequence/stakes]. Therefore, the central question is how to [decision/action direction] under [conditions]. In practice, a shorter version often suffices for beginning analysis: The problem is [structured condition], not merely [surface symptom], because [reason or sustaining structure], which matters because [stakes].

2.8 When Problem Framing May Not Serve

The method of deliberate problem framing described in this chapter is not always the right approach. Under severe time pressure — a medical emergency, an active system failure, a moment requiring immediate response — the cost of framing may exceed its value. In such situations, the brain’s rapid pattern recognition, heuristics, and trained intuition may serve better than explicit linguistic analysis. The deliberate framer must recognize when the situation demands action over analysis.

Problem framing also has limits when the situation is not yet stable enough to be framed. A problem that is still unfolding, where key facts are actively changing, may resist the kind of structure this chapter recommends. Premature framing can lock thinking into a structure that reality soon outgrows. In such cases, the thinker may need to hold multiple provisional frames simultaneously rather than committing to one.

Finally, not every important problem can be fully captured in language. Some decisions involve tacit knowledge, embodied skill, or deep intuition that resists explicit formulation. Framing is valuable, but it is not a substitute for the kind of understanding that comes from experience and cannot be fully stated. The disciplined thinker uses framing as a tool, not as a cage, and knows when to set the tool down.

2.9 Chapter Summary

This chapter has argued that problem framing is the first act of deliberate language. A problem is not simply given by reality; it is constructed through language by defining an object, boundary, standard, cause, agent, consequence, and direction. Different formulations produce different paths of thought and action, which means that how a problem is stated is already an act of judgment.

A weak frame may confuse symptoms with causes, compress complexity into labels, assign blame too early, assume false choices, or smuggle solutions into the problem statement before the issue has been examined. Common failure modes include the label frame, the symptom frame, the blame frame, the false choice frame, the totalizing frame, and the solution-first frame.

A strong frame makes the issue thinkable, bounded, testable, and actionable. It distinguishes symptom from cause, sets appropriate boundaries, identifies the standard behind the problem, names assumptions, and reveals the action path implied by the formulation. The purpose of framing is not to make a problem sound better; it is to make the problem structurally available to judgment.

2.9.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied in every act of deliberation.

First, a problem is not found but constructed. The way you state a problem determines what solutions become visible. Do not treat surface complaints as problems; dig until you reach the structured condition that requires attention. Second, a complete problem frame contains eight elements: object, boundary, condition, standard, cause, agent, consequence, and direction. A frame that omits any of these may lead reasoning astray. Third, distinguish symptoms from causes. Acting on a symptom without identifying its cause produces shallow intervention that does not address the underlying issue. Fourth, test your frame with alternatives. The first frame you produce is rarely the best one. Ask: what does this frame make visible, what does it hide, and what alternative formulation might reveal more? Fifth, identify the standard behind the problem. Without an explicit standard, there is no basis for evaluating whether the problem has been resolved. These five principles form the foundation of all subsequent deliberation. Master them before moving on to the next task: clarifying what you are trying to achieve and what standards will govern your choice, which is the subject of Chapter 2.

2.9.2 🎯 行动指南

今日练习：把一个模糊担忧转化为完整问题框架

找一句你最近经常说（或心里想）的抱怨式句子，比如”我没时间”、“团队效率太低”、“我找不到方向”。把它当作原材料，用本章的八要素框架重新构建：

  1. 对象(Object)：这个问题的核心是什么？不是宽泛的”时间”，而是具体的什么？
  2. 边界(Boundary)：这个问题覆盖什么范围？排除什么？
  3. 状态(Condition)：当前的实际情况是什么？用可观察的语言描述。
  4. 标准(Standard)：和什么期望比才成了问题？谁制定的这个标准？
  5. 原因(Cause)：什么在制造或维持这个状态？区分症状和根源。 6 行动者(Agent)：谁能做什么来改变它？你在其中有多少控制力？
  6. 后果(Consequence)：如果不处理，会有什么影响？对谁？
  7. 方向(Direction)：什么样的改变是需要的？不是一个解决方案，而是一个方向。

完成之后问自己：这个新框架让我看到了什么之前没看到的东西？如果我只做一件事来改善这个状况，那应该是什么？

本周挑战：在三次日常对话中，注意别人说”问题在于X”的时候——X是症状还是结构？在心里重新构建一遍。
