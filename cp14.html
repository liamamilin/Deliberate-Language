15  Strategy, Sequencing, and Leverage

15.1 Beyond the Plan

A plan organizes action. A strategy organizes action in relation to priority, sequence, leverage, resistance, timing, and future position. A plan may tell us what to do; strategy tells us why this order matters, which action has the highest structural value, where effort should be concentrated, and how one move prepares the next.

This chapter moves beyond planning into strategic thinking. The previous chapter showed how language turns decision into plan. This chapter studies how language organizes priorities, sequence, leverage points, and strategic structure. A plan may say: first do A, then do B, then do C. A strategy asks: why A first, what does A unlock, what does B depend on, which action creates the most leverage, which action reduces the main constraint, and which action improves our future position?

Strategy is not merely a larger plan; it is a way of thinking about action as a structured movement through constraints. A weak strategy says “We will work hard on many important things.” A stronger strategy says “We will focus first on the bottleneck that limits all later progress; once that is solved, the next actions become easier, cheaper, and more effective.” The difference is leverage.

15.2 Strategy as Direction Under Constraint

Strategy begins when a goal meets constraint. If there were no constraints, strategy would be unnecessary because everything could be done at once. But real action is constrained by time, money, energy, attention, capability, information, social permission, institutional rules, competition, uncertainty, and risk. Strategy exists because not everything can be done at once, so it must ask: given the goal and the constraints, what should receive priority?

Priority is the language of strategic importance. To prioritize is not merely to choose what is important; many things may be important. Priority means deciding what matters first because it has the greatest effect on the whole structure. A strategic question is: which action, if done first, makes later actions easier, cheaper, or more effective? This is different from asking which task is most urgent; urgency and strategic importance are often different dimensions.

Sequence is central to strategy because order changes outcome. Sequencing by dependency means doing first what other actions depend on. Sequencing by risk means testing the most uncertain assumption early, before large commitments have been made. Sequencing by learning means arranging actions so that each step produces information that improves the next step. Sequencing by capability means building skills or resources before they are needed. A strategically sequenced plan does not just list tasks in a convenient order; it arranges them so that each action prepares the ground for the next.

A bottleneck is a constraint that limits the throughput of the entire system or the progress of the entire plan. Identifying the real bottleneck is a strategic act because removing it produces the greatest improvement per unit of effort. Effort spent on non-bottleneck activities, while potentially valuable in isolation, does not accelerate the overall progress.

15.3 Leverage

Leverage is the relationship between input and effect. A leverage point is an action, decision, capability, resource, or position that produces effects beyond its immediate size. Strategic language looks for leverage by asking: which action unlocks several later actions, which capability compounds over time, which bottleneck if removed improves the whole system, and which early investment reduces future cost?

Different types of leverage operate in different domains. Structural leverage comes from changing a system’s rules, incentives, or architecture rather than working within them. Capability leverage comes from building a skill or knowledge that transfers across multiple contexts. Information leverage comes from knowledge that reduces uncertainty or reveals hidden opportunities. Relationship leverage comes from connections that open doors, provide support, or enable coordination. Timing leverage comes from acting when conditions are most favorable. Positioning leverage comes from occupying a strategic place in a network or market.

Leverage is different from effort. Hard work without leverage produces limited results; small actions at the right leverage point can produce disproportionate effects. Strategic thinking distinguishes between tasks that consume time and tasks that create structure, between actions that feel productive and actions that actually change the trajectory.

A strategically coherent plan aligns actions with the goal, sequences them to build on each other, concentrates effort on leverage points, excludes actions that do not serve the direction, and adapts based on feedback. Strategic focus means not only doing the right things but also excluding the wrong things. Strategic exclusion is harder than strategic addition because it requires saying no to opportunities that are valuable but not aligned with the current priority.

15.4 Strategy Language Patterns

Good strategy language names the bottleneck, identifies the leverage point, explains the sequencing logic, and distinguishes strategic actions from reactive ones. It says: “We will focus first on X because it is the bottleneck; once X is resolved, Y becomes easier and Z becomes possible.”

Bad strategy language treats all tasks as equally important, confuses urgency with priority, reacts to pressure without direction, or operates without a clear theory of how actions lead to outcomes. It says “We need to do everything” which is not strategy but its absence.

Strategy is a theory of action: it proposes that if we do X in this order under these conditions, Y will result. This theory can be tested, confirmed, or disproven by the feedback that emerges during execution. A strategic thinker does not only execute the plan but also watches for signals that confirm or challenge the theory.

15.5 Chapter Summary

This chapter has studied strategy as the organization of action according to priority, sequence, leverage, and direction under constraint. Strategy is not merely a larger plan; it is a way of thinking about action as structured movement through constraints.

Priority determines what matters first; sequence determines the order that prepares each next step. Leverage identifies points where small inputs produce large effects. Bottlenecks are the constraints that limit overall progress. Different types of leverage operate in different domains: structural, capability, information, relationship, timing, and positioning.

Good strategy language names the bottleneck, identifies leverage, sequences by dependency or risk, and maintains coherence between actions and goals. Bad strategy language confuses urgency with priority or treats all tasks as equally important.

15.5.1 Essential Knowledge

The following principles must be retained and applied whenever you design a strategy.

First, strategy begins when a goal meets constraint. If you could do everything, you would not need strategy. Because you cannot, you must choose what matters first. Second, distinguish priority from urgency. Urgent tasks demand immediate attention but may not be strategically important. Strategically important tasks may not be urgent. Let the goal, not the loudest pressure, determine your priority. Third, identify the bottleneck. The bottleneck is the constraint that limits the progress of the entire plan. Removing it produces more improvement per unit of effort than any other action. Effort spent on non-bottleneck activities, however valuable in isolation, does not accelerate overall progress. Fourth, seek leverage points. A leverage point is where a small input produces a large effect: an action that unlocks several later actions, a capability that compounds across contexts, or information that reduces critical uncertainty. Fifth, sequence deliberately. Sequence by dependency (do first what others depend on), by risk (test the most uncertain assumption early), by learning (arrange actions so each produces information for the next), and by capability (build skills or resources before they are needed). Sixth, strategy is a theory of action. It proposes: if we do X in this order under these conditions, Y will result. Watch for signals that confirm or challenge this theory. When reality contradicts the theory, adapt the strategy, not the goal.
